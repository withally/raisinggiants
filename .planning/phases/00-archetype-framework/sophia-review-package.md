## 2. Scoring Dimensions

The framework uses 11 scoring dimensions. Each quiz answer contributes to one or more dimension scores (1–10 scale), and the user's dimension profile is then matched to the 9 archetype reference profiles via weighted similarity distance.

**All dimensions scored 1–10:** 1–3 = Low, 4–6 = Mid-range, 7–10 = High.

---

### Dimension 1: Emotional Warmth
**Research anchors:** Baumrind (1967) Responsiveness axis; Gottman (1997) Emotion Coaching; PBDQ Factor 1; Ainsworth (1978) Sensitive Responsiveness; Kennedy (2022)

**Description:** The degree to which a parent provides physical and verbal affection, emotional acceptance, and consistent emotional availability. Measures not just whether warmth is felt internally but how readily and explicitly it is expressed — through words, touch, attunement to emotional states, and responsive comfort.

**Low (1–3):** Affection primarily expressed through provision, reliability, and practical care rather than verbal or physical warmth. Love is shown by doing, not by saying.

**High (7–10):** Openly and frequently expressive of love and acceptance. Comfortable with physical affection and verbal reassurance. Warmth feels like a natural, ongoing part of the relationship.

> **Review prompt:** Is "Emotional Warmth" the right name and scope for this dimension? Does the low/high description feel accurate?

---

### Dimension 2: Boundary Consistency
**Research anchors:** Baumrind (1967) Demandingness axis; Maccoby & Martin (1983); PBDQ Factor 5 (Permissive Discipline, inverse); PBDQ Factor 6 (Democratic Discipline)

**Description:** The degree to which a parent sets clear, predictable expectations and follows through on them consistently. Measures not just whether rules exist but whether they are reliably enforced, age-appropriately explained, and applied without large fluctuation based on the parent's mood.

**Low (1–3):** Rules shift frequently or are rarely enforced. Children learn that limits are negotiable. Structure feels unpredictable or absent.

**High (7–10):** Clear, consistent expectations. When a limit is set, it is held — kindly but firmly. Children experience the parent as reliable and predictable.

> **Review prompt:** Is "Boundary Consistency" the right framing? Is the distinction from Protective Instinct clear enough — a parent can be anxiously over-controlling (high PI) but inconsistent with rules (low BC)?

---

### Dimension 3: Autonomy Support
**Research anchors:** Baumrind (1967) Authoritative parenting as autonomy-granting within structure; PBDQ Factor 4; Tsabary (2010) Conscious Parenting

**Description:** The degree to which a parent scaffolds the child's independence and self-determination rather than making decisions for them, over-directing their choices, or fostering dependence.

**Low (1–3):** Tends to step in, solve, or decide rather than let the child figure things out. May experience the child's choices as reflective of the parent's own competence or identity.

**High (7–10):** Actively creates space for the child to try, fail, and try again without rescuing. Comfortable with age-appropriate risk. Curious about the child's own preferences and choices.

> **Review prompt:** Is the low end well-distinguished from Protective Instinct (high PI = anxious monitoring; low AS = over-directing)? Are these truly measuring different constructs?

---

### Dimension 4: Emotional Regulation and Coaching
**Research anchors:** Gottman (1997) Emotion Coaching vs. Emotion Dismissing; Siegel (2010) Window of Tolerance; Tsabary (2010) Conscious vs. Reactive; Siegel & Hartzell (2003); Kennedy (2022)

**Description:** Two tightly coupled constructs: (a) how well the parent manages their own emotional activation under stress, and (b) whether the parent coaches the child's emotions (validates, names, helps process) or dismisses/punishes them.

**Low (1–3):** Tends to react when emotionally activated. Child's big feelings feel threatening. May dismiss ("you're fine"), minimise ("stop crying"), or inadvertently punish emotional expression.

**High (7–10):** Stays regulated enough to stay present when the child is upset. Names feelings, validates without needing to fix. Treats the child's emotional expression as a window and an opportunity.

> **Review prompt:** Is combining self-regulation and emotion coaching as a single dimension clinically sound? Could they diverge enough that they need to be separate dimensions?

---

### Dimension 5: Protective Instinct and Vigilance
**Research anchors:** PBDQ Factor 3 — Anxious Intrusiveness; Ainsworth (1978) Anxious/Ambivalent Attachment; van IJzendoorn (1995)

**Description:** The degree to which a parent's behaviour is organized around preventing harm, monitoring for threats, and maintaining control out of anxiety rather than curiosity. Clinically distinct from Boundary Consistency: a parent can be low on BC and high on PI (anxiously hovering without clear rules).

**Low (1–3):** Comfortable with child taking age-appropriate risks. Does not spend significant mental energy monitoring for threats. Trusts the child's environment and other caregivers without persistent anxiety.

**High (7–10):** Strong, persistent drive to protect. Significant mental energy devoted to threat monitoring and harm prevention. May find it very difficult to allow age-appropriate risk. Child's safety is a dominant organising concern.

> **Review prompt:** Is the mid-range (4–6) well-specified as "healthy adaptive protective drive" vs. the high end as "anxious intrusiveness"? Is the threshold clear enough for quiz question design?

---

### Dimension 6: Narrative Coherence About Own Past
**Research anchors:** Siegel (2010) Autobiographical integration; Main (1984) Adult Attachment Interview; Siegel & Hartzell (2003); van IJzendoorn (1995) meta-analysis

**Description:** How coherently and with what level of emotional integration a parent can describe and make sense of their own childhood experience. Key finding: it is not the quality of the childhood but the coherence of the story that predicts parenting quality.

**Low (1–3):** Difficulty telling a coherent, nuanced story about childhood. May idealise, dismiss, or become preoccupied. Narrative has gaps, contradictions, or emotional flooding.

**High (7–10):** Can tell a nuanced, emotionally accessible story about their own childhood — including both positive and difficult experiences — with contextual understanding. The story has a beginning, middle, and end. Has "made sense" of the past.

**Open question for Sophia:** Should Narrative Coherence apply to the Current lens scoring, or remain a Past-lens-only dimension?

> **Review prompt:** Is the Narrative Coherence dimension scoreable via self-report quiz questions? Are candidate questions around "describe your childhood in five minutes" or "has your thinking about your parents become more nuanced over time" likely to surface genuine coherence vs. idealisation/preoccupation?

---

### Dimension 7: Presence and Attunement
**Research anchors:** Tsabary (2010, 2014) Conscious Parenting; Siegel (2010) Mindsight; Gottman (1997); Gottman Institute bids for connection

**Description:** The degree to which a parent is genuinely present and attuned to the child's actual emotional state vs. parenting from their own agenda, unmet needs, or unconscious projections. This dimension captures the awakened-vs-automatic axis.

**Low (1–3):** Frequently parenting on autopilot. Own emotional needs, anxieties, or unfinished business from childhood regularly colours perception of the child's behaviour.

**High (7–10):** Able to pause, observe, and genuinely ask "what is my child experiencing right now?" before responding. Parenting is responsive to the child's actual state, not filtered through the parent's projection.

**Open question for Sophia:** Is this dimension sufficiently distinct from Emotional Regulation? Both involve regulation, but PA is about attuned responsiveness to the child's present state, while ER is about the parent's self-regulation and coaching behaviour.

> **Review prompt:** Should Presence/Attunement remain a separate dimension from Emotional Regulation, or are they too correlated to be useful as separate scoring axes?

---

### Dimension 8: Repair and Reconnection
**Research anchors:** Kennedy (2022) — repair as the cornerstone of secure attachment; Tronick (2007) — Still Face Paradigm; Gottman Institute — repair attempts; Siegel & Bryson (2011)

**Description:** The capacity to repair ruptures in the parent-child relationship — to return after conflict, name what happened, and actively rebuild the connection. Research shows that the ratio of successful repairs to unrepaired ruptures is a stronger predictor of relationship quality than the frequency of conflict itself.

**Low (1–3):** Ruptures tend to be left unaddressed — conflict ends when the storm passes, not through active reconnection. The child learns that breaks in the relationship must be forgotten rather than processed.

**High (7–10):** Consistently returns after rupture — names what happened, takes responsibility for their part, and checks in on the child's experience. Repair is a deliberate practice. Comfortable saying "I got that wrong" or "I was too harsh."

> **Review prompt:** Is Repair and Reconnection a distinct enough dimension from Emotional Regulation and Warmth? A parent can be warm and regulated but still never return to name a rupture. Does this distinction resonate clinically?

---

### Dimension 9: Role Integrity
**Research anchors:** Jurkovic (1997) — parentification; van der Kolk (2014) — relational trauma; Siegel & Hartzell (2003); Bowlby (1969/1982)

**Description:** Whether appropriate parent-child role boundaries exist around emotional responsibility. Measures the degree to which the parent carries the emotional weight of the relationship rather than the child carrying it for them. Low Role Integrity is the signature of parentification — the child appearing mature and responsible while carrying emotional weight that belongs to the parent.

**Low (1–3):** The child carries emotional responsibility that belongs to the parent — managing the parent's moods, providing comfort during the parent's distress, mediating family conflicts. The child may appear competent but at developmental cost.

**High (7–10):** The parent carries the emotional responsibility of the relationship. The child is free to be a child without worrying about the parent's state. Role boundaries are clear even when the parent is struggling.

> **Review prompt:** Is Role Integrity a clinically useful dimension for a self-report quiz? Many parents who are engaged in parentification patterns are not consciously aware of them. How would you recommend framing questions to surface this without triggering defensive responses?

---

### Dimension 10: Reciprocity
**Research anchors:** Grusec & Davidov (2010) — domain-specific socialization; Duncan et al. (2009) — mindful parenting; PBDQ Factor 6; Maccoby (2007)

**Description:** The degree to which a parent treats the parent-child relationship as a genuinely bidirectional exchange — one in which the child's perspective, preferences, and input are actively sought and incorporated. Distinct from Autonomy Support (which measures the child's freedom to act independently) — Reciprocity measures the parent's willingness to be influenced by the child within the relationship itself.

**Low (1–3):** Family decisions flow one direction — from parent to child. The child's opinions may be heard but do not materially influence outcomes. Disagreements are resolved by parental authority rather than negotiation.

**High (7–10):** Actively seeks the child's input on decisions that affect them. Comfortable being influenced by the child's perspective and adjusting plans accordingly. Models that relationships are cooperative.

> **Review prompt:** Is the Reciprocity dimension clinically distinct from Autonomy Support? In practice, can a parent be high on one and low on the other? Is this distinction useful for archetype differentiation?

---

### Dimension 11: Nonjudgmental Acceptance
**Research anchors:** Rohner (2004) IPARTheory; Kennedy (2022) — "good kid having a hard time"; Rogers (1951) — Unconditional Positive Regard; Gottman (1997)

**Description:** The degree to which a parent communicates unconditional positive regard for the child — acceptance of who the child is, independent of the child's behavior, achievements, or compliance with parental expectations. Distinct from Emotional Warmth (which measures the quantity and expressiveness of affection) — Nonjudgmental Acceptance measures whether that warmth persists when the child disappoints, fails, or deviates from expectations.

**Low (1–3):** Love and approval are visibly linked to behavior and achievement. The child experiences warmth as something that fluctuates based on performance. May use withdrawal of affection or disappointment as motivational tools.

**High (7–10):** The child experiences love as a constant — present even in moments of failure, defiance, or disappointment. The parent can be firm about behavior while communicating clearly that the child's worth is not in question.

> **Review prompt:** Are all 11 dimensions necessary? Should any be combined, split, renamed, or removed? Are any so correlated with another dimension that they are measuring the same underlying construct?

**Foundational Patterns**
*Headline: Your calm, consistent presence gives your children a deep sense of safety they carry into the world.*

- Emotional predictability — your children always know what to expect from you, and that reliability builds secure attachment from the inside out (Baumrind, 1967)
- Boundaries as an act of love — your clear structure communicates care and safety, not control or restriction
- Modelling regulation under pressure — you demonstrate that difficult emotions can be felt without being acted on, and your children internalise this as their own capacity (Siegel & Hartzell, 2003)
- Warmth and firmness together — you hold both at once, which research identifies as the most protective combination for long-term child wellbeing
- The safe harbour — your children return to you knowing they will be received without judgment, which frees them to explore and take risks

**Research anchor:** Baumrind (1967) authoritative framework; Siegel (2003) "safe haven" as foundation of secure attachment

**Watchouts**
*Headline: When stress runs high, your steadiness can tip into emotional distance — and the people closest to you may feel it first.*

- Emotion-dismissing under pressure — high self-regulation can inadvertently suppress rather than process difficult emotions (Gottman, 1997 "emotion-dismissing" pattern); children may learn to hide feelings to preserve the calm
- Missing bids for connection — when focused on maintaining equilibrium, you might respond to the surface of what your child says rather than the emotional undercurrent beneath
- Rigidity masking as consistency — your reliable structure can harden into inflexibility under stress; the situation calls for adaptation but you default to the familiar pattern
- Under-expressing warmth to those outside the immediate family — your capacity to hold it together can mean that partners, extended family, or the children themselves receive less emotional availability
- Difficulty receiving support — the anchoring role can make it harder to let others hold you; consistent caregivers often defer their own needs in ways that compound over time

**Research anchor:** Gottman's emotion-dismissing pattern; Siegel's "river of integration" — rigidity at one end, chaos at the other

**Foundational Patterns**
*Headline: Your children know, with absolute certainty, that you will move mountains to keep them safe — and that certainty is its own kind of love.*

- Love as protection — your care is expressed through vigilance and provision; your children feel held even when they can't articulate why
- Clear and consistent expectations — your children always know where the lines are, and that predictability builds a particular kind of trust
- Unwavering commitment — when the world is difficult or threatening, your children know you won't flinch; your loyalty is a bedrock resource
- Practical reliability — you show up, you follow through, you handle it; this action-based care is deeply felt even when it goes unremarked
- High standards as belief in potential — your expectations come from a place of deep investment in your children's outcomes

**Research anchor:** Ainsworth (1978) protective availability as secure attachment component; Lansford et al. (2024) cross-cultural protective parenting outcomes

**Watchouts**
*Headline: When worry is running the show, your fierce protection can become the thing that keeps your children from finding their own strength.*

- Autonomy erosion — well-intentioned protection that removes challenge also removes experiences through which children build self-efficacy and resilience; when stressed, you might find yourself solving problems your child was ready to solve themselves (PBDQ anxious intrusiveness)
- Emotional scanning replacing emotional attunement — protective vigilance focuses outward on threats; you might be assessing risk rather than receiving your child's inner experience, leaving them feeling watched rather than seen
- Fear transmission — parental anxiety is contagious; children of highly vigilant parents often develop their own threat-sensitivity without a corresponding sense of their own competence
- Control as default response — when a situation feels uncertain, you might add more structure and oversight rather than tolerating the ambiguity that learning requires
- Difficulty distinguishing real risk from imagined risk — high protective instinct can over-generalise to low-risk situations, narrowing the space available for your children to grow

**Research anchor:** PBDQ anxious intrusiveness; Ainsworth anxious/ambivalent attachment; Siegel on unprocessed parental fear transmission

**Foundational Patterns**
*Headline: Your children grow up knowing that their inner world matters, and that knowledge becomes the foundation of their emotional intelligence.*

- Emotional fluency — you create a home where feelings are named, received, and worked through rather than managed or suppressed; this emotional vocabulary is one of the most protective gifts a childhood can offer
- Genuine presence — you are truly with your children when you are with them; you notice their emotional state and respond to what they are actually feeling
- Autonomy and trust — you believe in your children's capacity to navigate their own experience; you offer support without taking over, which builds genuine self-direction over time
- Non-judgment as invitation — your children tell you things because they trust you won't immediately fix, lecture, or react; that trust is earned through consistent emotional availability
- High emotional intelligence inheritance — children raised with consistent emotional attunement develop stronger empathy, better peer relationships, and greater emotional self-regulation (Gottman, 1997)

**Research anchor:** Gottman (1997) emotion-coaching; Siegel (2010) "mindsight" — the capacity to see and be seen in relationship

**Watchouts**
*Headline: When love and limits feel like opposites, your warmth can leave your children without the structure they need to feel truly safe.*

- Boundaries dissolving under emotional pressure — the same attunement that makes you responsive to feelings can make it hard to hold a limit when they're distressed; when stressed, you might find yourself negotiating your way out of a boundary you already set (Baumrind, 1971 permissive parenting)
- Comfort prioritised over growth — your natural instinct is to reduce your child's discomfort; this can communicate that difficult feelings are dangerous
- Inconsistency creating ambient anxiety — children with permissive parents often report feeling anxious even in very warm environments because unpredictable limits create a felt sense of instability
- Difficulty tolerating your own discomfort at their discomfort — when your child is unhappy, your instinct to soothe may be partly about managing your own distress
- High emotional support, low emotional challenge — your children know they can come to you with how they feel, but may not always get the gentle pushback that helps them develop frustration tolerance

**Research anchor:** Baumrind (1971) permissive parenting; Gottman's "laissez-faire" parent who acknowledges emotions but fails to set limits

**Clinical pattern:** Scaffolding/conscious. Very high autonomy support + high presence/attunement + good emotional regulation + minimal protective instinct. Characteristic of Tsabary's conscious parenting model: deliberate, growth-oriented, choosing responses rather than reacting. Warmth is genuine but more intellectualised than emotionally spontaneous. Deliberate repair practice — intentionally returns and names ruptures. Signature: intentional, growth-minded, very comfortable with the child's independence.

**Foundational Patterns**
*Headline: You approach parenting as one of the most conscious and transformative acts a person can undertake — and that intention shapes everything you do.*

- Deliberate responsiveness — you pause between trigger and response; this gap is where your parenting lives, and research identifies it as the space where intergenerational patterns either repeat or change (Tsabary, 2010)
- Scaffolding independence — you see your role as building your child's capacity to navigate their own life; you offer support that develops their competence rather than substituting for it
- Curiosity about self and child — you approach parenting with genuine inquiry, asking not just "what do I do?" but "what is driving this behaviour?" and "what is this moment asking of both of us?"
- Growth orientation — you genuinely believe both you and your children are always capable of learning, changing, and expanding; mistakes are data, not verdicts
- Trust in the child's process — you are deeply comfortable with your children having experiences that are theirs, making decisions that are theirs, and learning from consequences that are theirs

**Research anchor:** Tsabary (2010) conscious parenting; Siegel (2010) mindsight as capacity to pause, observe, and choose

**Watchouts**
*Headline: When parenting becomes a project to perfect rather than a relationship to inhabit, your thoughtfulness can create distance you didn't intend.*

- Over-intellectualising emotion — research on conscious parenting shows a risk of analysing feelings rather than feeling them; when stressed, you might find yourself thinking through an emotional moment rather than being inside it with your child
- Warmth that requires effort — your attunement is real but often effortful; in moments of depletion or surprise, you may find yourself reaching for the thoughtful response rather than having it arise naturally, which can be felt as slight distance
- The perfectionism of intentionality — the same drive that makes you reflective can turn your parenting into a performance you're grading yourself on; you might be more focused on whether you responded correctly than on how your child is doing
- Difficulty with spontaneity — your comfort zone is the considered response; you may find it harder to be playful, messy, or unpredictably warm — the moments that build relational intimacy through sheer delight
- Moderate narrative integration — intellectual understanding of one's past does not always translate into visceral emotional integration; some of your own story may still be operating outside your awareness (Siegel & Hartzell, 2003)

**Research anchor:** Siegel (2003) distinction between intellectual understanding and true narrative integration; Tsabary's caution that conscious parenting can become another ego performance

**Foundational Patterns**
*Headline: You bring your whole self to parenting — your warmth, your sensitivity, and your genuine commitment to growing alongside your children.*

- Emotional generosity — you give freely from your heart; your children experience a parent who is genuinely present with their feelings and responds to the texture of their emotional life, not just its surface
- Attuned responsiveness — you notice what your children are actually experiencing and respond to that; this attunement is a cornerstone of secure attachment (Ainsworth, 1978)
- Growth as a shared practice — you model that adults are also works in progress; your children grow up understanding that learning and changing are not just things children do
- Warmth under pressure — your emotional availability doesn't disappear when things are hard; your children know they can come to you when they are struggling, which keeps the relationship open through difficulty
- Heart-led engagement — you approach your children with curiosity and care rather than strategy or agenda; this creates the quality of relationship that children remember most clearly when they look back on their childhoods

**Research anchor:** Ainsworth (1978) consistent emotional availability as secure attachment foundation; Gottman (1997) attuned responsiveness as strongest predictor of emotional intelligence

**Watchouts**
*Headline: When your own story is still finding its shape, you might find that your children's emotional moments sometimes activate yours — and the line between their experience and yours can blur.*

- Emotional resonance becoming emotional absorption — your attunement means you feel your children's feelings with them; research on less-integrated caregivers shows this can tip from empathy into merger, where your child's distress becomes your distress and your response is driven by your own activation rather than their need
- Unprocessed past surfacing in present moments — low narrative coherence predicts being "triggered" by specific child behaviours; when stressed, you might find yourself responding to a present moment with an emotional intensity that belongs to your own history (Siegel & Main)
- Protection and warmth narrowing autonomy — your care for your children is deep and genuine; combined with a still-integrating history that includes ambient anxiety, you might find yourself wanting to keep your children close in ways that subtly limit their development of independence
- Inconsistent structure — your warmth is steady but your boundaries may move with your emotional state; when well-resourced, limits are clear; when activated or depleted, they may soften in ways that create unpredictability
- Drawn to parenting as healing — there is something beautiful about approaching parenting as a growth process; the watchout is when the child's role in the parent's healing narrative becomes more prominent than the child's own developmental story

**Research anchor:** Main & Solomon (1990) unresolved/disorganised states as predictor of inconsistent parenting; Siegel & Hartzell (2003) "emotionally reactive" parent with lower narrative integration

*Headline: When love and expectation become the same thing, your children may grow up feeling that they are loved for what they achieve rather than who they are.*

**Research anchor:** Rohner (2004) cross-cultural research — perceived conditional acceptance; Assor, Roth & Deci (2004) emotional costs of conditional regard

- Unconditional acceptance as foundation — your children experience love that does not fluctuate with their behavior or achievements; this stable acceptance frees them to take risks, make mistakes, and develop authentically without fear of losing your regard

- Democratising what shouldn't be democratic — not all decisions benefit from shared power; safety decisions and situations requiring parental expertise may be undermined by a reflexive move toward collaboration; the child needs to know that some things are simply decided
- Role clarity under pressure — the egalitarian orientation can blur the generational boundary that children need to feel safe; when stressed, the collaborative dynamic may create a peer-like quality that leaves the child uncertain about who is actually in charge
- Difficulty with firm limits — holding a hard limit without negotiation may feel like a betrayal of your relational values; children need the experience of encountering a limit that does not move, as this builds frustration tolerance
- The acceptance trap — your deep nonjudgmental acceptance is a genuine strength, but under stress it can slide toward accepting behaviors that genuinely need to be challenged; the distinction between accepting the child and accepting all their choices can blur

**Research anchor:** Baumrind (1971) — permissive parenting; Duncan et al. (2009) — nonjudgmental acceptance must coexist with appropriate limit-setting
