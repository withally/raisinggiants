# KOL Synthesis: Parenting Research Frameworks and Dimension Mapping

**Created:** 2026-02-24
**Status:** Baseline for Sophia's clinical review
**Purpose:** Cross-reference major parenting researchers' frameworks to identify and justify the scoring dimension space used in the Raising Giants archetype system.

This document is the bedrock research artifact for Phase 0. The chosen scoring dimensions must be traceable to this synthesis. No archetype dimension profile should be authored until this table is reviewed and the dimension set is confirmed clinically.

## 1. KOL Synthesis Table

Each row covers: framework name, key dimensions contributed, whether a validated psychometric instrument exists, and relevance to the product's three-lens model (Past / Current / Aspirational).

| Researcher | Framework | Key Dimensions Contributed | Validated Instrument? | Relevance to Three-Lens Model |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Diana Baumrind** | Parenting Styles (1967, 1971) | Responsiveness (warmth, emotional availability); Demandingness (structure, control, rule-enforcement) | Parenting Styles and Dimensions Questionnaire (PSDQ) — widely used, peer-reviewed | The core 2D model underpins the entire dimension space. Responsiveness maps to Emotional Warmth; Demandingness maps to Boundary Consistency. Past lens captures how parents were raised on these axes; Current captures how they parent now. |
| **Maccoby & Martin** | Extended 4-quadrant model (1983) | Added the Permissive (high responsiveness, low demandingness) and Neglectful (low both) quadrants to Baumrind's Authoritative/Authoritarian | Extension of Baumrind's PSDQ | Confirms that low responsiveness AND low demandingness is a clinically distinct pattern (not just "low Baumrind"). Important for recognizing disengaged parenting without pathologizing it. |
| **John Gottman** | Emotion Coaching (1997) | Emotional awareness of child's feelings; Empathic attunement; Coaching emotions rather than dismissing or pathologizing them | Parenting Style Questionnaire (Gottman Lab); observational coding | Directly maps to Emotional Regulation / Coaching dimension. Also informs Emotional Warmth. Critical for understanding how a parent responds to their child's distress — a key quiz measurable. Current lens especially. |
| **Daniel Siegel** | Interpersonal Neurobiology / Mindsight (2010) | Mindsight (capacity to see one's own and child's inner mental life); Emotional integration across time; Narrative coherence (ability to tell a coherent story about one's own past) | No single quiz instrument — primarily clinical/observational | Provides the theoretical basis for the Past lens. A parent who can tell a coherent, integrated story of their own childhood (even a difficult one) tends to transmit secure attachment. Narrative Coherence is the dimension most uniquely tied to the three-lens model. |
| **Mary Ainsworth** | Attachment Theory — Strange Situation (1978) | Secure attachment (responsive, available caregiver); Anxious/Ambivalent (inconsistent); Avoidant (dismissing, emotionally distant); Disorganized (frightening or frightened) | Strange Situation Procedure (observational lab protocol, not a quiz) | Foundational for understanding why past experiences predict current parenting. Attachment style of the parent's own childhood predicts their parenting behavior via intergenerational transmission. Informs Protective Instinct (anxious pattern), Emotional Warmth (secure vs. avoidant). |
| **Mary Main** | Adult Attachment Interview — AAI (1984) | Coherent Narrative About Past (how the adult describes their childhood, regardless of content); Earned Secure (insecure childhood but coherent adult integration); Unresolved (trauma/loss markers disrupting narrative) | Adult Attachment Interview (AAI) — structured clinical interview, not a self-report quiz | The AAI is the gold standard for measuring how adults have made sense of their own upbringing — exactly what the Past lens aims to capture. Narrative Coherence as a dimension is directly derived from Main's work. Key finding: it is not the quality of childhood but the coherence of the story that predicts parenting quality. |
| **Shefali Tsabary** | Conscious Parenting (2010, 2014) | Presence vs. Projection (parenting from present-moment awareness vs. projecting own unmet needs onto child); Awakened vs. reactive parenting | Popular framework; clinically adjacent but not a validated psychometric instrument | Maps directly to the Presence / Attunement dimension. Tsabary distinguishes conscious, intentional parenting from unconscious, reactive parenting — a dimension that captures whether parents respond to the child's actual emotional state or their own projected needs. |
| **Daniel Siegel & Mary Hartzell** | Parenting from the Inside Out (2003) | Autobiographical Narrative Integration (making sense of one's childhood story to parent differently); Emotional reactivity vs. response | Book + clinical framework; no standalone instrument distinct from Siegel/AAI | Reinforces Narrative Coherence and bridges it to Emotional Regulation. Specifically addresses the mechanism: parents who have not integrated their own stories tend to be "triggered" by their children's behaviors in ways that parallel their own childhood patterns. Important for the Past-to-Current arc. |
| **PBDQ (Loeber et al. / Louwerse et al.)** | Parenting Behavior and Dimensions Questionnaire (2011) | 6 empirically-derived factors: (1) Emotional Warmth, (2) Punitive Discipline, (3) Anxious Intrusiveness, (4) Autonomy Support, (5) Permissive Discipline, (6) Democratic Discipline | PBDQ — validated peer-reviewed psychometric instrument (PMC4456141) | Most directly useful framework for this product. Six factors are empirically grounded, mutually distinguishable, and measurable via Likert-scale self-report questions. Maps cleanly onto the recommended dimension set. Emotional Warmth (factor 1), Anxious Intrusiveness (factor 3 → Protective Instinct), Autonomy Support (factor 4) are adopted directly. |
| **Jennifer Lansford et al.** | Cross-Cultural Parenting Studies (ongoing, PMC11542638) | Cultural variation in parenting warmth, control, and discipline; within-culture variance exceeds between-culture variance; context-contingency of parenting effects | Not a single instrument — meta-analytic synthesis across multiple cultures | Grounds the cultural overlay system. Key finding: no parenting dimension is universally "good" or "bad" — effects are context-contingent. Protective/controlling parenting is more strongly linked to positive outcomes in contexts where external threat is real. Justifies treating cultural overlays as expression modifiers, not archetype shifts. |
| **Marinus H. van IJzendoorn** | Intergenerational Transmission Meta-Analysis (1995, PMC3060612) | Intergenerational concordance (adult attachment → infant attachment): ~75% secure-to-secure; ~70% insecure-to-insecure; Earned Secure (EQ: coherent narrative despite adverse history) | AAI + Strange Situation as paired instruments | Provides the empirical grounding for soft inference: when only the Past lens is completed, the Current/Aspirational inference is justified by this ~75% transmission rate. Also establishes Narrative Coherence as the mechanism: coherent story-telling about the past = higher probability of secure parenting regardless of past content. |

**Notes on merging decisions:**

- **Punitive Discipline (PBDQ factor 2)** is NOT included as a standalone dimension. It correlates strongly with inverse Emotional Warmth and inverse Emotional Regulation and adds collinearity without distinctive explanatory power for archetype differentiation. It is subsumed into the Boundary Consistency watchout content.
- **Permissive Discipline (PBDQ factor 5)** is captured as the low end of Boundary Consistency rather than a separate dimension — the product's spectrum model handles this via the scale (low Boundary Consistency = permissive).
- **Earned Secure (van IJzendoorn)** is a state, not a dimension — it describes the outcome of high Narrative Coherence despite adverse past, not a separate measurable construct. Captured in Narrative Coherence scoring and content.
- **Disorganized Attachment (Ainsworth)** is not directly modeled as a dimension because it is clinically complex and difficult to operationalize via self-report quiz without feeling invasive. The associated patterns (high Protective Instinct + low Emotional Regulation) will appear as a recognizable archetype profile combination.

## 3. Recommended Scoring Dimensions

Based on the synthesis above, 7 dimensions are recommended. These represent constructs that: (a) appear in multiple validated frameworks, (b) are mutually distinguishable, and (c) can be operationalized via natural-feeling self-report quiz questions.

### Dimension 1: Emotional Warmth

**Canonical name:** Emotional Warmth
**Appears in:** Baumrind (Responsiveness), Gottman (Empathic attunement), PBDQ (Factor 1), Ainsworth (Secure availability)
**What it measures:** The degree to which a parent provides physical and verbal affection, emotional acceptance, and consistent emotional availability. High scores reflect openly expressive warmth; low scores reflect warmth expressed primarily through provision and action rather than emotional language.
**Why included:** Single most cross-validated dimension in the parenting literature. Present in every major framework. Directly measurable via quiz questions that feel natural and non-clinical.

### Dimension 2: Boundary Consistency

**Canonical name:** Boundary Consistency
**Appears in:** Baumrind (Demandingness), Maccoby & Martin (high/low demandingness axis), PBDQ (inverse of Factor 5, aspects of Factor 6)
**What it measures:** The degree to which a parent sets clear, predictable expectations and follows through on them consistently. High scores reflect firm, reliable structure; low scores reflect variable or absent rule-enforcement.
**Why included:** The second axis of the foundational Baumrind model. Essential for distinguishing authoritative (high warmth, high consistency) from permissive (high warmth, low consistency). Directly measurable via behavioral self-report.

### Dimension 3: Autonomy Support

**Canonical name:** Autonomy Support
**Appears in:** Baumrind (Authoritative parenting), PBDQ (Factor 4), Tsabary (over-enmeshment vs. allowing child's selfhood)
**What it measures:** The degree to which a parent scaffolds the child's independence and self-determination versus fostering dependence or enmeshment. High scores reflect encouraging exploration and self-directed decision-making; low scores reflect over-involvement in the child's choices.
**Why included:** Clearly distinguishes authoritative (autonomy-supporting) from authoritarian (autonomy-controlling) parenting patterns even when both exhibit high Boundary Consistency. Measurable but requires careful question framing to avoid social-desirability bias.

### Dimension 4: Emotional Regulation and Coaching

**Canonical name:** Emotional Regulation and Coaching
**Appears in:** Gottman (Coaching vs. Dismissing), Siegel (Emotional integration), Tsabary (Conscious vs. reactive), Siegel & Hartzell (Autobiographical narrative integration and triggered reactivity)
**What it measures:** Two tightly coupled constructs measured as one dimension: (a) how well the parent manages their own emotional activation (especially under stress), and (b) whether the parent coaches the child's emotions (validates, names, helps process) or dismisses/pathologizes them. High scores reflect regulated, coaching response; low scores reflect reactive, dismissing, or punitive response to the child's emotional expression.
**Why included:** Gottman's research shows this is the single best predictor of child emotional intelligence. The coupling of self-regulation and coaching is intentional — parents who cannot regulate their own emotions cannot coach their children's. Both are measured together because quiz questions naturally capture them jointly.

### Dimension 5: Protective Instinct and Vigilance

**Canonical name:** Protective Instinct and Vigilance
**Appears in:** PBDQ (Factor 3: Anxious Intrusiveness), Ainsworth (Anxious/Ambivalent attachment)
**What it measures:** The degree to which a parent is driven by anxiety about harm, overprotection, or intrusive involvement motivated by fear rather than curiosity. High scores reflect parents whose parenting is strongly organized around preventing harm and maintaining control; low scores reflect parents with a more relaxed, exploratory approach to risk. Mid-range scores capture the clinically healthy protective instinct that all good parenting includes.
**Why included:** Clinically significant as a dimension that cuts across warmth levels — a parent can be both high warmth AND high anxious intrusiveness. Measurable via questions about letting children take risks, monitoring, and worry patterns.

### Dimension 6: Narrative Coherence About Own Past

**Canonical name:** Narrative Coherence
**Appears in:** Siegel (Mindsight; narrative integration), Main (AAI coherence scale), Siegel & Hartzell (Parenting from the Inside Out), van IJzendoorn (AAI coherence as predictor of infant attachment)
**What it measures:** How coherently and with what level of integration a parent can describe and make sense of their own childhood experience. High scores reflect a parent who can tell their childhood story — even if it was painful — with emotional access, chronological consistency, and without becoming lost in or dismissing that story. Low scores reflect either idealization (vague positives without evidence) or unresolved preoccupation (overwhelmed by the narrative).
**Why included:** This is the dimension most directly tied to the product's core mechanic — the Past lens. The research finding (Main, van IJzendoorn) is that it is not the quality of the childhood but the coherence of the story that predicts parenting quality. This dimension is the mechanism behind intergenerational transmission. Note: applies most directly to the Past lens; Current/Aspirational lens questions would use different framing.

### Dimension 7: Presence and Attunement

**Canonical name:** Presence and Attunement
**Appears in:** Tsabary (Presence vs. Projection), Siegel (Mindsight; present-moment attunement), Gottman (Emotional awareness)
**What it measures:** The degree to which a parent is genuinely present and attuned to the child's actual emotional state versus parenting from their own agenda, unmet needs, or projections. High scores reflect conscious, responsive parenting that tracks the child's present state; low scores reflect reactive parenting organized around the parent's own emotional needs. This dimension captures the "awakened vs. automatic" axis that Tsabary describes.
**Why included:** Adds a dimension that Baumrind's framework does not capture — a highly structured, warm parent can still be systematically attuned or systematically projecting. Distinguishes the Authoritative archetype from a conscious/mindful variant. Operationalizable but requires careful question framing to avoid spiritual language that might alienate some users.

## 4. Excluded Dimensions

The following dimensions were considered and excluded with reasons:

| Dimension Considered | Source | Reason for Exclusion |
|---|---|---|
| **Punitive Discipline** | PBDQ Factor 2 | Collinear with inverse Emotional Warmth and inverse Emotional Regulation. Does not add discriminant validity for archetype differentiation. Captured in content as a watchout for relevant archetypes. |
| **Democratic Discipline** | PBDQ Factor 6 | Substantially overlaps with both Boundary Consistency (high end) and Autonomy Support (high end). Not independently measurable without creating redundant quiz questions. |
| **Permissive Discipline** | PBDQ Factor 5 | Captured as low Boundary Consistency. A separate dimension would confuse users and produce questions that feel repetitive. |
| **Intergenerational Trauma (explicit)** | Clinical literature generally | Clinically valid but cannot be measured via self-report quiz without feeling invasive or clinically loaded. Trauma-related patterns emerge naturally from the combination of low Narrative Coherence + low Emotional Regulation scores and are addressed in archetype content rather than as a dimension. |
| **Socioeconomic Stress as Parenting Modifier** | Lansford cross-cultural research | Real moderator but not a parenting dimension — it is a context variable, not a parenting behavior dimension. Would be collected as demographic data, not scored as a dimension. |
| **Attachment Style (labeled)** | Ainsworth | The four attachment classification labels (Secure/Anxious/Avoidant/Disorganized) are research constructs measured via observational protocols, not self-report. Using the labels directly creates clinical loading and good/bad hierarchy risks. The underlying behavioral dimensions (Emotional Warmth, Protective Instinct, Emotional Regulation) capture attachment without the clinical labels. |
