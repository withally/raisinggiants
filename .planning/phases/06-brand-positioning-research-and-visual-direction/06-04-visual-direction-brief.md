## Positioning Anchor

The brand territory, locked in Plan 06-03, is: **digital tools for parents who want to understand themselves — not just their children.**

This territory was discovered, not pre-committed. It emerged from mapping sixteen millennial parenting digital products and finding a specific, unoccupied gap: the entire millennial parenting digital market serves child management. No competitor addresses the millennial parent as a psychological being with their own history, patterns, and interiority. The whitespace is not aspirational — it is structural. No one is there.

The emotional core of the brand sits at the intersection of three truths:

1. **The past shapes the present.** What you received as a child — the parenting style, the emotional environment, the spoken and unspoken rules — is active in you today. Most people sense this. Almost none have language for it.
2. **Recognition, not improvement.** The brand's value proposition is revelation: "I always sensed this. Now I see it." This is not therapy. Not healing. Not growth. It is the specific, profound satisfaction of seeing something clearly that you have always known was there.
3. **Self-understanding as the starting point.** Whether the product is The Mirror (understanding your upbringing), The Blueprint (building from that understanding), or The Partner Match (extending understanding to your most formative adult relationship), the brand root is the same: turning the lens inward, on yourself, not on your children.

**What the visual identity must achieve:** Before a user reads a single word, the visual language must signal: *depth, intimacy, and the kind of intelligence that meets you at the level of your actual sophistication.* It must not signal: therapy, wellness, parenting advice, entertainment, or the generic personality-quiz register.

**What it must NOT do:**
- Build visual identity around any single product metaphor (the mirror, the blueprint, the match) — these are product signals, not brand signals
- Use child-coded visuals (bright primaries, cartoon illustration, playful type) — these signal child-facing products
- Read as "startup warm neutral" (generic amber, startup teal, wellness purple)
- Use imagery or tone that implies the user needs fixing

## Section 1: Color Palette

### Current State Assessment

The current palette:
- Primary backgrounds: `amber-50` (~`#FFFBEB`), `white`, `stone-900` (~`#1C1917`)
- Accent: `amber-600` (~`#D97706`) / `amber-700` (~`#B45309`) / `amber-400` (~`#FBBF24`)
- Text: `stone-900`, `stone-600`, `stone-500`

Per 06-RESEARCH.md: "reads 'startup warm neutral' rather than 'emotionally distinctive.'" The amber is functional but not semantically meaningful — it doesn't communicate anything specific about this brand's territory. Warmth is present; specificity is not.

**What is being replaced and why:**
- `amber-600` / `amber-700` / `amber-400` → Replaced with terracotta. Amber is a generic "warm startup" color with no specific semantic claim. Terracotta encodes the past, the earthen, the enduring — precisely what this brand is about.
- `amber-50` background → Replaced with bone (`#F7F2EB`). Bone reads as aged parchment, not generic off-white.
- `stone-900` text → Replaced with warm charcoal (`#1C1512`). Stone-900 has a cool undertone that reads as institutional; warm charcoal reads as grounded and human.
- Pure white `#FFFFFF` → Not used. Clinical, tech, institutional.

### 1.1 Primary Palette

**The three primary colors appear on every product across the brand family.** They are the ambient visual air of the brand — the colors users see before they see anything else.

---

#### Primary 1 — Bone
**Hex:** `#F7F2EB`
**Color name:** Bone
**Role:** Primary page background, card backgrounds, body of landing pages

**Emotional rationale:** Warmer and more aged than white; more specific than generic beige. Bone reads as parchment — the material of notebooks, letters, books kept over time. This is the color of the surfaces on which important things are recorded. For a brand about understanding what you carry from your past, the background color should feel like the paper on which memory is preserved, not the blank white of a digital interface or the generic cream of a template. Bone is non-clinical, non-startup, and specifically analog in register.

**Multi-product test:**
- The Mirror: Bone creates the contemplative background against which the archetype revelation lands. Warm enough to feel intimate; specific enough to not read as quiz app.
- The Blueprint: Bone reads as the material of a physical blueprint — the off-white drafting paper on which plans are drawn. Semantically reinforces the product name without requiring it.
- The Partner Match: Bone provides neutral warmth for a product dealing with emotionally charged relationship territory — neither clinical nor excessively cozy.

---

#### Primary 2 — Warm Charcoal
**Hex:** `#1C1512`
**Color name:** Warm Charcoal
**Role:** Primary text, headlines, high-contrast elements across all products

**Emotional rationale:** Slightly warmer than Tailwind stone-900 (which has a cool undertone). Near-black with warmth communicates: serious, grounded, present — not institutional, not clinical, not tech-product. The warmth in the undertone is the difference between a text that reads as "I am a product" and one that reads as "I am worth reading slowly." The cool undertone of stone-900 sub-consciously registers as modern-digital; warm charcoal registers as printed matter, literary, permanent.

**Multi-product test:**
- Universal: This color functions as the text color across all products. Its warmth prevents the brand from reading as tech/clinical regardless of product context. Works on bone, soft clay, and deep dusk backgrounds.

---

#### Primary 3 — Terracotta
**Hex:** `#B05A3A`
**Color name:** Terracotta
**Role:** Primary CTAs, active states, accent marks, archetype labels, key typographic emphases

**Emotional rationale:** This is the semantic anchor of the entire palette — the single chromatic identity of the brand. Terracotta is earth that has been fired: permanent, specific, past. The word itself means "baked earth" (Italian). For a brand whose territory is "understanding the ground on which you were built" — the formative conditions of childhood, the patterns that persist into adulthood — terracotta is not a decorative choice. It is the most direct available chromatic encoding of this territory.

Terracotta is not orange (energetic, startup, tech). Not red (urgent, emotional, alarming). Not amber (generic warm, startup kit). Terracotta is grounded. It is the color of clay vessels, of Mediterranean walls, of soil that has been here long enough to have a name. Multiple reference brands in the 06-02 gallery (Big Life Journal, Gottman Institute) successfully use terracotta without coding as "children's brand" — disambiguation comes from typography and illustration register, not from color.

**Multi-product test:**
- The Mirror: Terracotta on archetype labels creates the "vocabulary gift" moment — the named experience arriving in earthen, permanent-feeling color. The color encodes "this is true and has been true" not "this is urgent and exciting."
- The Blueprint: Terracotta as CTA color ("Start your Blueprint") sits in the semantic context of "building from the ground" — earthy, foundational.
- The Partner Match: Terracotta avoids the "compatibility quiz" warm-pink register that would undermine the product's revelation framing.

**Avoidance note:** Replace `amber-600` completely and immediately. No amber in any interface that uses terracotta. The two cannot coexist without the amber making terracotta look like an error.

### 1.2 Secondary / Accent Palette

**Secondary colors support the primary palette and enable product differentiation within the brand family.** They are used less frequently and more intentionally than primary colors — they mark moments of insight, architectural depth, and product-specific identity.

---

#### Secondary 1 — Aged Gold
**Hex:** `#C4892A`
**Color name:** Aged Gold
**Role:** Researcher/evidence emphasis, "insight arrival" moments, secondary highlight states

**Emotional rationale:** Where terracotta is the past (the earth, the formative ground), aged gold is accumulated understanding — wisdom earned over time. Gold implies something refined by duration, not applied by decoration. This is emphatically not Tailwind `amber` (which reads as startup cheerfulness) — aged gold is deeper, more saturated toward ochre, and carries the visual weight of something old. Used for secondary emphasis: researcher names in the KOL credibility section, archetype labels in context (distinct from terracotta primary CTAs), "now I see it" revelatory text moments.

**Product differentiation potential:** Aged gold can serve as a product accent for The Blueprint specifically — associating the concept of "accumulated wisdom turned into a forward-looking plan" with a gold-toned accent. This creates visual distinction between The Mirror (terracotta-dominant) and The Blueprint (gold-leaning) without breaking brand coherence.

**Multi-product test:**
- Brand level: Aged gold as the evidence/insight accent works across all products — research grounds all three products equally.
- The Mirror: Gold on researcher names in results section. Archetype names on result page.
- The Blueprint: Gold as product-specific accent if product-level differentiation is needed at launch.
- The Partner Match: Gold used sparingly; this product should lean on terracotta-dominant palette to avoid "precious" register.

---

#### Secondary 2 — Soft Clay
**Hex:** `#EBE0D5`
**Color name:** Soft Clay
**Role:** Card surfaces, secondary section backgrounds, subtle depth layers between bone sections

**Emotional rationale:** A slightly darker variant of bone — clay, not parchment. Provides depth in layout without introducing new chromatic hues. Where bone is the primary page surface (the air the content breathes in), soft clay is the material of elevated surfaces — cards, call-out boxes, inset content. The clay-bone relationship is the same as the relationship between a desk and a book on the desk: both are analog, warm, non-digital in feel. This creates the sense of textural layering that editorial publications use to organize hierarchy.

**Multi-product test:** Universal secondary surface. Works identically across The Mirror, Blueprint, and Partner Match because it introduces no product-specific semantic content.

---

#### Secondary 3 — Deep Dusk
**Hex:** `#1E1814`
**Color name:** Deep Dusk
**Role:** Dark section backgrounds for alternating layout rhythm; high-contrast hero moments; the most authoritative content statements

**Emotional rationale:** A deeper, warmer version of stone-900. On deep dusk: text in bone, accents in aged gold — the gold reads as "earned authority," the bone reads as "documentary record." The warmth of deep dusk (versus stone-900's cool undertone) prevents the dark from reading as tech-dark or premium-cold. Used for the most important content statements — the research grounding section, the "what we know" moments in the product.

**Product differentiation potential:** Deep dusk sections can carry different content across products while maintaining brand consistency through the color. The Mirror uses deep dusk for researcher names and research evidence. The Blueprint could use deep dusk for the most structured, directive content. The Partner Match for the most intimate relational content.

## Section 2: Typography

### 2.1 Display Font: Cormorant Garamond

**Decision: KEEP — but dramatically increase italic utilization.**

This is not a passive "keep" — it is an active decision based on the 06-02-A finding and the 06-03 positioning narrative requirements.

**Rationale:**

Cormorant Garamond is a calligraphic serif with high stroke contrast, generous descenders, and — most critically — an italic form of unusual expressive depth. The swash capitals, deeply inclined text, and delicate hairline strokes in italic create emotional expressiveness that very few display typefaces achieve. Reference brand pattern synthesis (06-02) confirms: "editorial serif with high stroke contrast and generous ascenders" is the consistent description of what works for the depth register targeting millennial parents.

**Why not change:**
1. **06-02-A confirmed:** "Aligned with all high-depth reference brands" — The New Yorker, The Atlantic, The School of Life, and Kinfolk all use this exact typographic register.
2. **The italic form has not been exploited.** The problem with the current implementation is underuse, not wrong font. Cormorant Garamond has been used primarily in regular weight for large headlines. The italic — its strongest feature — appears rarely. The fix is application, not replacement.
3. **Implementation cost of replacement outweighs any marginal benefit** of switching to Canela or Freight Display — neither would produce meaningfully better results for this territory.

**What must change — application rules:**

**Weights to use:**
- Regular (400): Section opener headlines, large display text
- Italic (400i): Pull quotes, subheadings in personal/intimate contexts, emotional emphasis statements, archetype names in prose context
- NOT Bold — Cormorant's elegance is its stroke contrast, not its weight. Bold forms fight the font's natural quality. If emphasis is needed, use italic or size, never bold.

**Size hierarchy:**
- Hero headline: 72–96px regular or italic (not bold). The large size is the impact — not weight.
- Section openers: 48–64px regular. First lines of major sections signal "you are beginning something."
- Subheadings: 28–36px italic. The italic form here signals: "you are now in a personal, direct conversation" — not a structural header.
- Pull quotes / revelation moments: 24–32px italic, centered, line height 1.85. This is the typography of "I always sensed this, now I see it."
- Minimum size: 22px. Below this, Cormorant's hairline strokes disappear. Never use Cormorant Garamond below 22px.

**Specific use rules:**
- ALWAYS use italic for: archetype names appearing in prose ("you are a Resilient Striver"), emotional callout sentences, the revelation moment in product copy
- NEVER use: all-caps Cormorant (fights lowercase proportions), bold Cormorant, Cormorant below 22px, Cormorant for body text, Cormorant for UI labels or navigation

**Multi-product test:**
Cormorant Garamond's editorial register serves the entire brand family. The Mirror's revelation language ("you received the parenting of a Structured Mentor"), The Blueprint's considered-plan framing ("your Blueprint begins with what the Mirror revealed"), and The Partner Match's relational depth language all sit naturally in an editorial serif. The font is a brand-level signal, not a product-specific one.

---

### 2.2 Body Font: Geist Sans

**Decision: KEEP for marketing/interface — evaluate DM Sans for long-form result content.**

**Rationale:**

Geist Sans is Vercel's open-source font — clean, geometric-inflected, excellent at small sizes. For quiz questions, marketing copy, navigation, labels, and CTAs: Geist works well. It is neutral enough to not compete with Cormorant, legible at small sizes, and renders reliably across rendering environments.

**The single concern:** The Mirror's result page contains 800–1,200 words of archetype profile content that users are expected to read carefully and slowly. Geist's slight geometric rigidity may introduce reading fatigue in extended reading sessions that a humanist sans-serif (DM Sans, Inter) would not. Humanist sans-serifs have more organic proportions that reduce friction in long-form reading.

**Recommendation:** Keep Geist Sans as the primary body font for marketing copy and interface elements. If user testing reveals result-page reading fatigue, introduce DM Sans via `next/font/google` as a second body variable (`--font-body-long-form`) for result page body text only. This is a low-cost intervention that can wait for evidence before implementation.

**Multi-product test:** Geist Sans as body font is a neutral, functional choice that creates no product-specific semantic signal. It works identically across The Mirror, Blueprint, and Partner Match.

---

### 2.4 Typography Rules

**Cormorant Garamond rules:**
- ONLY used at 22px and above
- NEVER in bold weight
- NEVER in all-caps with letter-spacing
- NEVER for body text (below headline/pull-quote scale)
- NEVER for UI elements (buttons, nav, labels)
- ALWAYS the default for hero headlines and emotionally weighted display text

**Geist Sans rules:**
- Body text, UI elements, labels, CTAs, navigation
- NEVER for hero headlines or emotional display text
- Measure constraint: max-width of body text set to enforce 65–75 chars/line (approximately 680px at 17px)

**Pairing rule:** Cormorant makes the emotional claim; Geist delivers the information. Every content section should have a clear decision about which role it plays. Avoid using Geist for emotionally weighted headings or Cormorant for informational body text.

## Section 3: Imagery System

### 3.1 Brand-Level Illustration Direction

**The illustration system must encode the parent's interiority — their psychological inner life — not any product-specific metaphor.**

This is the single most important constraint on the imagery system. The Mirror's metaphor (reflection, seeing yourself), The Blueprint's metaphor (structure, building), and The Partner Match's metaphor (connection, dialogue) are all product-level metaphors. Brand-level illustration must be above all three. What is common to all three products is not their metaphors but their audience: the millennial parent who turns the lens inward, on themselves.

**Brand illustration subject matter:** The inner life of a reflective adult. The experience of looking back at your own childhood from a position of adult awareness. The moment of recognition — "I've always known this." Scenes and states that would be immediately recognizable to the audience but rarely depicted: sitting with a memory, looking at something that holds the past, the relationship between who you are now and who you were shaped to be.

**Target illustration register: Editorial line illustration — School of Life style**

The School of Life's illustration system is the strongest single reference. Characteristics:
- Ink line on warm ground (bone, ivory, cream)
- Moderate line weight — confident without being heavy; hand-drawn in feeling, not machine-precise
- Low to moderate detail — the metaphor communicates clearly without being over-explained
- Figures in states of interior experience: sitting alone, looking at something, in a moment of recognition
- No faces required — the absence of specific faces allows the viewer to project themselves into the scene
- Occasional single-color accent (one element in terracotta, one in aged gold) against line-art; not full color

**Visual references for illustrators:**
1. **The School of Life** (theschooloflife.com) — primary reference; the register, the emotional subjects, the line quality
2. **Kai Brammer** — editorial line illustration with emotional interiority; figures in contemplative states
3. **Olimpia Zagnoli** — warm, human editorial illustration; emotionally specific without photorealistic detail

**Scene types for brand-level illustration (not product-specific):**
- A figure looking at an object from childhood (a toy, a piece of furniture, a door) — recognition without nostalgia
- A person in a domestic interior, paused in thought — the private moment of awareness
- Two overlapping representations of the same figure: one present, one past — without literal before/after framing
- Hands — writing, holding something, reaching toward a remembered object
- A reflective surface showing not the present moment but something older — generic enough to not be "The Mirror" but evocative of looking back

---

### 3.2 Photography Direction

Photography is secondary to illustration for this brand. When used (social media, email, future editorial content):

**Subject matter (use):**
- Adults in private moments of thought — reading, sitting with a feeling, paused
- Hands engaged in writing, holding objects, in relationship with things that carry history
- Objects with age and history — worn notebooks, photographs being held, objects that have been kept
- Unguarded faces — not posed, not performing, not smiling at camera

**Subject matter (avoid):**
- Lifestyle photography (happy family, outdoor adventure, morning routine)
- Children as subjects (signals child-facing brand)
- Nature-as-metaphor (sunrises, mountains, water as emotional symbol — too generic)
- Posed couple photography (triggers "relationship advice" register)
- Group or family shots

**Style guidance:**
- Natural interior light — afternoon light through a window quality
- Warm color grading, slightly desaturated, slightly aged — not vintage-filter, not contemporary crisp
- Intimate framing — close, personal, not environmental portrait
- Generous negative space around subject

---

### 3.3 Brand Illustration vs. Product Illustration

This distinction is critical to maintaining multi-product brand coherence:

**Brand illustrations:**
- Appear on: landing page hero, shared brand materials, email headers, brand-level content
- Subject: parent interiority — the audience's inner life, not the product's function
- Register: School of Life editorial line — warm, contemplative, non-metaphor-specific
- Color: charcoal line on bone ground, maximum one terracotta accent element
- Purpose: create audience identification — "this is about someone like me"

**Product illustrations:**
- Appear on: individual product pages, product-specific marketing, results interface
- Subject: can encode product metaphors (reflection for The Mirror, architectural/structural for The Blueprint, dialogue/encounter for The Partner Match)
- Register: consistent with brand illustration style (same line quality, same palette) but with product-metaphor content
- Purpose: orient the user to the specific product's value proposition

**Anti-pattern being corrected:** The current `MirrorIllustration.tsx` is a product-level illustration (mirror/reflection metaphor) used at brand level (landing page hero). This should be redesigned as a brand-level illustration — a scene of interior experience that is recognizable to the audience without being tied to The Mirror product specifically. The Mirror product gets its own illustration on its product page.

**What is being replaced:**
- `MirrorIllustration.tsx` → Brand-level parent-interiority illustration (redesign required)
- `ArchetypeIcons.tsx` → Product-level: The Mirror archetype icons can be redesigned in the editorial line style (replacing current geometric SVG approach) without changing their function; this is a style update, not a structural change

## Section 4: Layout Approach

### 4.1 Whitespace Philosophy

**The brand requires generous whitespace as a trust signal.** Pattern synthesis from all ten reference brands (06-02) is unambiguous: whitespace is the most reliable visual signal of depth for this audience. Brands that registered as "deep" used significantly more whitespace than brands that felt shallow.

The target: closer to Kinfolk (radical editorial whitespace) than to Headspace (functional wellness-product spacing). Not Kinfolk's extreme silence, which would sacrifice warmth — but 30–50% more whitespace per section than the current implementation.

**Why whitespace is not emptiness:** Whitespace in editorial design is the typographic equivalent of pauses in a slow, meaningful conversation. It communicates: "we are not rushing you. This is worth your time." For a brand whose product asks users to slow down and look at their own upbringing — the layout must create the visual environment in which that kind of slow reflection is possible.

**Minimum specifications:**
- Section vertical padding: 80px minimum on mobile, 120px minimum on desktop
- Above-fold content: 60% viewport maximum — the hero should invite curiosity and leave room for the page to breathe
- Body text measure: max 680px width at 17px (enforces 65–75 chars/line)

---

### 4.2 Content Density

**Low density is the correct setting for this brand.** The editorial register requires that content not compete with itself. Each section should carry one idea, one emotional claim, one visual anchor — not a grid of benefits, not a feature comparison matrix.

**Specific prohibitions:**
- No 3-up or 6-up feature card grids — this is the single strongest signal of "startup quiz template"
- No progress bars styled as gamification elements
- No persistent sticky CTAs appearing within the first 300px of scroll
- No section-level tab navigation or menu-within-section patterns

**Specific affirmations:**
- Text sections run to their natural length — no "read more" truncation that prevents reading
- CTA placement feels earned — appears after the user has received enough to want to act
- Quiz interface feels like a private questionnaire, not a game — no animation between questions, no gamification signals

---

### 4.3 Section Rhythm

Each landing page section has a defined role in an editorial sequence. The rhythm pattern repeats once per 3–4 viewport heights:

1. **Opening narrative** — text-heavy, generous whitespace, Cormorant display headline on bone background. Establishes emotional territory. No competing images.
2. **Visual breathing** — illustration-anchored, minimal text, primarily bone/soft clay surface. Allows the previous section to settle.
3. **Evidence** — structured content (researcher names, citations, claims) with aged gold typography. Research grounding without institutional coldness.
4. **Intimate testimony** — pull quotes in Cormorant italic, personal framing, the vocabulary-gift moment. Users recognize themselves.
5. **Transition + CTA** — returns to text, introduces the product action, lands the decision in the emotional context established by the sequence.

**Background alternation:** Bone → Soft Clay → Bone → Deep Dusk → Bone. The Deep Dusk section should appear once per full page for the most authoritative content claim.

---

### 4.4 Responsive Priorities

The primary audience is millennial parents. Mobile usage patterns:
- Quiz experience: mobile-first — single-question-per-screen, touch-friendly targets (44px minimum), no horizontal scrolling
- Result reading: mobile needs careful reading experience — enforce character measure on mobile via max-width constraints; single column
- Landing page: mobile hero should show headline + subhead + CTA; illustration appears below fold

Desktop priorities:
- Landing page benefits from editorial two-column layouts (text left, illustration right or reversed) in evidence sections
- Result page: single column, generous side padding — even on desktop, the result content should feel like reading a letter, not a dashboard

## Section 5: Brand Tone and Voice

### 5.1 Tone Attributes

**1. Knowing**

The brand speaks as if it already knows something true about the person it is addressing — because it does. It has studied the research; it has mapped the patterns. It is not seeking engagement or offering advice. It is delivering recognition. A "knowing" tone does not guess or suggest — it names.

*Do:* "You've probably spent years aware that something about how you were raised is active in you today."
*Don't:* "Many adults wonder how their childhood affected them."

The "do" is knowing — it addresses the specific person, assumes the awareness is already there, gives it language. The "don't" is clinical and distancing — it generalizes, it suggests, it reduces.

---

**2. Revelatory**

The primary register of the brand is revelation: "I always sensed this. Now I see it." Not therapy, not growth, not healing — seeing. The tone should feel like the moment a sentence in a book makes you underline it because it named something you could not name.

*Do:* "The archetype framework gives you the words for what you've always felt without language."
*Don't:* "Discover your parenting archetype and start your growth journey."

The "do" is revelatory — it positions the product as delivering language for pre-existing awareness. The "don't" is discovery/growth register — it implies novelty and improvement, not recognition.

---

**3. Peer-level**

The brand speaks to adults as adults — intellectually serious, psychologically sophisticated, capable of nuance. Not a teacher with a student. Not a therapist with a client. Not a brand with a customer. A peer who has read the research and is offering what they found.

*Do:* "The research behind the Mirror is grounded in attachment theory and parenting science — Bowlby, Ainsworth, Gottman, Siegel. These aren't self-help frameworks; they're decades of empirical work."
*Don't:* "Our expert-backed quiz uses cutting-edge research to give you personalized insights!"

The "do" treats the user as someone who will find the source names meaningful. The "don't" uses credibility-by-assertion language that actually signals the opposite.

---

**4. Grounded**

Specific over general. Concrete over abstract. Named experience over described experience. The brand prefers "the people who raised you used predictability as a form of love — structure was how they showed care" over "your parents had a certain parenting style." The specificity is what creates recognition.

*Do:* "The Structured Mentor's household ran on predictability. You knew what was expected, and meeting those expectations was how connection happened."
*Don't:* "People raised by authoritative parents often have high achievement orientation."

The "do" names a specific, recognizable experience. The "don't" describes a category from outside the experience.

---

**5. Permission-giving**

Before any revelation, give permission. This pattern appears across Brené Brown, On Being, and getrelatio.com — it creates psychological safety for the depth the brand requires. The structure:
1. Name what the user might be sensing before they have language for it
2. Normalize: confirm this sensing is common, real, worth attending to
3. Deliver the named concept as a vocabulary gift
4. Invite action from a position of trust already established

*Example:* "You've probably spent years aware that something about how you grew up shaped who you are. The way you react under pressure. What makes you feel safe — or suddenly, inexplicably, not. You've felt it without quite having words for it. The Mirror gives you those words."

---

### 5.2 Voice Register

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Clinical ←→ Personal | 8/10 toward Personal | "You" throughout. Never "many adults," "some people," "research suggests that individuals." Second-person direct is non-negotiable. |
| Expert ←→ Peer | 6/10 toward Peer | The brand has expertise (the research is real). But it delivers it peer-to-peer — as someone who found something and wants to share it, not as an authority handing down conclusions. |
| Prescriptive ←→ Revelatory | 9/10 toward Revelatory | The brand does not tell users what to do. It shows them what is already true. The one exception: The Blueprint product, which is inherently forward-looking — but even Blueprint language should frame guidance as "built from what you now understand" not "here is what you should do." |
| Serious ←→ Light | 7/10 toward Serious | Not humorless — but the humor is wry recognition, not warmth-performance. The brand is doing serious work about something real. It respects the subject matter and the user's actual experience. |

---

### 5.3 Vocabulary Guidance

**Words the brand uses:**
- "Sensed" (not discovered, found, or realized) — pre-existing awareness given language
- "Named" / "naming" — the vocabulary gift mechanism
- "Patterns" (not behaviors, traits, or tendencies) — patterns are emergent from conditions, not fixed character flaws
- "Shaped" (not caused, determined, or made) — shaped implies agency still present
- "Recognized" / "recognition" — the emotional state the brand produces
- "Carried" — "patterns you've been carrying" — implies weight, presence, and the possibility of understanding

**Words the brand avoids:**
- "Cycle-breaking" — this is cultural context (demand evidence) not brand vocabulary; therapy-adjacent and may age
- "Heal" / "healing" — positions the brand as remediation, not revelation
- "Trauma" in primary messaging — valid in product content where contextually appropriate, never in primary brand messaging
- "Discover" — implies novelty; the brand is about recognition of what was already there
- "Journey" — wellness-platform register; avoid unless in very specific contexts where it means "an insight journey" not a "growth journey"
- "Transform" / "empowerment" — aspiration register; incompatible with revelation register
- "Share your result" / "what type are you" — quiz/entertainment register
- "Break the cycle" — therapeutic framing; accurate demand signal, wrong brand register
- "Better parent" — outward-facing, implies the product is about parenting performance

---

### 5.4 Tagline Options

The brand territory ("digital tools for parents who want to understand themselves") suggests a tagline that lives at the intersection of the past, the self, and the act of naming.

The internal creative standard — "I always sensed this. Now I see it." — is the emotional experience the brand delivers. Tagline options should evoke this without being literal.

**Option 1: "Finally, the words for it."**
Rationale: "Words for it" encodes the vocabulary-gift mechanism. "Finally" encodes pre-existing awareness. Short, confident, personal. Works across all three products without requiring product-specific modification.

**Option 2: "What was already true."**
Rationale: The revelation register — "now I see it" implies "it was always there." Slightly more abstract than Option 1. Intellectually precise; may require more copy support to land.

**Option 3: "See what shaped you."**
Rationale: More explanatory than the others. "See" encodes the Mirror metaphor without requiring the Mirror product name. "Shaped" carries the brand vocabulary ("shaped by your upbringing"). Works as a product descriptor and a brand line simultaneously. Risk: slightly more clinical than Options 1 and 2.

**Option 4: "You've always known."**
Rationale: The most permission-giving of the options. Speaks directly to the user's pre-existing awareness. Creates intimacy immediately. Risk: slightly presumptuous — assumes the user knows, which may not feel earned before they've engaged with the product.

**Option 5: "For the parent who looks inward."**
Rationale: The most direct positioning statement — names the audience and their orientation explicitly. Extremely clear about who this is for and who it's not. Risk: too literal as a tagline; better as a positioning statement in copy than as a headline-level brand line.

**Recommended direction:** Options 1 and 2 are strongest as brand-level taglines. Option 3 functions well as a hero-level product descriptor that can sit below a brand tagline. Use Option 1 ("Finally, the words for it.") as the primary tagline direction for testing.

## Mood Reference

A user arrives on the Imprint landing page. The background is bone — not white, not beige, but parchment — and the first thing they see is a large Cormorant Garamond headline in warm charcoal, set loosely with generous line height: *"You've always sensed that something about how you were raised is still active in you."* The type is large enough that it asks to be read slowly. There is real space around it — not filler, not template spacing, but the space a sentence earns by being worth sitting with.

Below the headline, a single brand illustration: a figure in editorial line-art on the bone ground, drawn in warm charcoal ink. The figure is paused — looking at something just out of frame, in a moment of recognition, not action. A single terracotta element (a doorframe, a surface the figure is reaching toward) draws the eye without demanding it. The illustration is not trying to be beautiful. It is trying to be specific to the interior experience of an adult who has always sensed something they could not name.

As the user scrolls, the first section gives them language they recognize: the named patterns, the vocabulary for things they've carried without words. The terracotta accent appears on product labels — "The Mirror," "The Blueprint" — punctuating the text the way a teacher underlines a key term. On a deep dusk section, the researcher names appear in aged gold: Bowlby, Ainsworth, Gottman, Siegel. The gold reads as earned authority — these are not credentials for credibility theater; they are the sources of the framework's actual substance.

The result of this sequence — reading unhurried, moving through sections that alternate between text and breathing space, encountering an illustration that depicts something interior — is a feeling that precedes action: *this was made for someone like me. This knows something I haven't said out loud.*

The brand's visual system is complete when it produces this feeling without requiring any single sentence to carry the weight alone.
