# Visual Direction Brief
## Phase 06-04 — Translating Grounded Self-Discovery into Visual Language

**Date:** 2026-02-27
**Status:** Complete
**Positioning anchor:** The brand occupies the 'grounded self-discovery' territory — deep + warm, research-credible but emotionally intimate. The visual identity must make someone feel 'this understands me' before they read a word. Every visual choice must be testable against the emotional core: "I always sensed this. Now I see it."
**Feeds into:** 06-05-PLAN.md (rebrand execution plan)

---

## Positioning Anchor

The brand territory is grounded self-discovery — the intersection of clinical credibility and emotional intimacy. "Grounded" carries three simultaneous meanings: research-backed (the insight comes from clinical science, not astrology), the past as foundation (the product is about the ground on which you were built), and not abstract (specific, connected to reality). "Self-discovery" specifies the inward orientation — the adult reflecting on their own upbringing, not prescribing anything to others.

The emotional proposition is "I always sensed this. Now I see it." This is revelation, not validation or improvement. The product shows you something that was always true — it does not improve you, comfort you, or give you a type you can share. This creates a very specific visual mandate: the identity must communicate depth, intimacy, and a kind of earned intelligence — not wellness warmth, not clinical authority, not entertainment.

The brand is not: therapy (no healing journey language). Not wellness (no habits or practices). Not parenting advice (no prescriptive outward guidance). Not a personality quiz (not entertaining, not shareable-type-adjacent). Every visual choice that risks reading as any of these four categories must be reconsidered.

## Color Palette Direction

### Current State Assessment

The current palette uses:
- Primary backgrounds: `amber-50` (warm cream, ~`#FFFBEB`), `white`, `stone-900` (deep charcoal, ~`#1C1917`)
- Accent: `amber-600` (~`#D97706`) / `amber-700` (~`#B45309`) / `amber-400` (~`#FBBF24`)
- Text: `stone-900`, `stone-600`, `stone-500`

Per the 06-RESEARCH.md assessment: "reads 'startup warm neutral' rather than 'emotionally distinctive.'" The amber is functional but not specifically meaningful — it doesn't communicate anything unique about this product's territory. Warmth is there; specificity is not. The palette is directionally correct (non-clinical, non-purple, non-neon) but lacks a chromatic anchor that carries the emotional weight of "grounded self-discovery."

### Evaluating the Two Paths

**Path A: Deepen and differentiate the existing amber palette**

Strategy: Replace the generic amber with a more specific, emotionally charged variant — aged gold or deep amber — and introduce a secondary warm accent to differentiate from generic startup palettes. Preserves continuity with existing brand equity (however nascent), minimal disruption to codebase.

Evidence from reference brands: The School of Life uses deep mustard/golden yellow as a signifier of "considered time and intellectual depth." Day One uses warm gold as a "book spine" color. Neither is Tailwind's `amber` family — both are richer, more saturated, and carry more historical/emotional resonance.

Verdict: Path A can work, but only if the amber is *deepened* from the current `amber-600` (which reads startup) to a more specific aged-gold that carries genuine semantic weight. The distinction between Tailwind amber and aged-gold is not aesthetic — it is the difference between "warm" and "warm with a specific emotional claim."

**Path B: Shift to a more distinctive chromatic identity**

Strategy: Move the accent color entirely to a different hue — most likely terracotta/clay, which carries unique semantic content for this brand territory: earthy, past-oriented, enduring, specific.

Evidence from reference brands: The Gottman Institute uses warm terracotta as its human/earthy accent alongside a clinical-warmth neutral. In the color pattern synthesis across all 10 brands, "terracotta (the earthen, the past, the enduring)" is identified as one of the strongest candidates for a brand whose territory is "looking back at the ground on which you were built." Terracotta specifically encodes: soil, clay, something fired and permanent, the physical substance of the earth. For a product about understanding the formative conditions of your upbringing — the literal ground you were built on — this is not a decorative choice but a semantic one.

Verdict: Path B is the stronger recommendation based on what the positioning narrative establishes. The positioning is about the past as foundation, about the ground on which you stand. Terracotta is the most semantically direct chromatic encoding of this territory.

### Recommendation: Path B with Amber Background Continuity

Keep the warm cream/ivory backgrounds (which are working and are not generic) and the deep stone/charcoal text (which reads as intelligent depth, not clinical coldness). Replace amber accent with terracotta as the primary chromatic accent. This preserves what is working (the warm neutral base, the editorial depth of the near-black) while introducing a chromatic signal that is specific to the brand's positioning rather than generic to the "warm startup" category.

### Recommended Palette

**1. Bone (primary background)**
- Hex: `#F7F2EB`
- Role: Primary page background, card backgrounds
- Emotional rationale: Warmer and more aged than Tailwind amber-50. Reads as parchment, aged paper — the material substance of memory and record. "Bone" is neither clinical white nor ostentatiously warm. It has the texture of something that has been here a long time, which is what the product is about.

**2. Charcoal (primary text)**
- Hex: `#1C1512`
- Role: Primary text, headlines, high-contrast elements
- Emotional rationale: Slightly warmer than Tailwind stone-900 (which has a cool undertone). Near-black with warmth communicates: serious, grounded, present — not institutional, not clinical. The warmth prevents reading as tech product; the depth prevents reading as soft wellness.

**3. Terracotta (primary accent)**
- Hex: `#B05A3A`
- Role: Primary CTAs, active states, accent marks, key typographic emphases
- Emotional rationale: This is the semantic anchor of the palette. Terracotta is earth that has been fired — permanent, specific, past. For a brand about understanding the conditions that formed you, terracotta is the most direct chromatic claim: this product is about the ground. It is not orange (energetic, startup), not red (urgent, emotional), not amber (generic warm). Terracotta is grounded.

**4. Aged Gold (secondary accent)**
- Hex: `#C4892A`
- Role: Highlight states, supporting decorative elements, researcher/evidence emphasis
- Emotional rationale: Where terracotta is the past (the earth), aged gold is the accumulated understanding (the insight). Gold implies something that has been earned over time — wisdom, not luck. Used for secondary emphasis: researcher names, archetype labels, "finally seeing" moments in the product. Distinct from Tailwind amber because it reads as earned, not applied.

**5. Stone (supporting neutrals)**
- Hex: `#7A6E65` (mid), `#B8AFA6` (light)
- Role: Body text on light backgrounds, muted labels, supporting hierarchy
- Emotional rationale: Stone is the tonal background of the palette — not warm enough to be earthy, not cool enough to be clinical. The mid and light stone tones provide hierarchy without competing with terracotta and gold for chromatic attention.

**6. Deep Dusk (section alternate background)**
- Hex: `#1E1814`
- Role: Dark sections for alternating layout rhythm, high-contrast hero moments
- Emotional rationale: A deeper, warmer version of the current stone-900. Used for sections where the product speaks with its most authoritative, intimate voice — "here is what we know about your upbringing." The warmth prevents the dark from reading as tech-dark or premium-cold. On deep dusk backgrounds: text in bone, accent in aged gold.

**7. Soft Clay (secondary surface)**
- Hex: `#EBE0D5`
- Role: Card surfaces, secondary section backgrounds, subtle differentiation from primary bone
- Emotional rationale: A slightly darker variant of bone — clay, not parchment. Creates depth in the layout without introducing new chromatic hues. Communicates the same earthen, analog quality as bone but with more presence.

### Color Relationships and Rhythm

**Dominant:** Bone (background), Charcoal (text) — the reading surface. These appear on every page and should feel like the natural visual air of the brand.

**Structural accent:** Terracotta — used sparingly but consistently as the single chromatic through-line. One element per section maximum. Never background; always foreground element (button, underline, archetype label, icon).

**Earned accent:** Aged Gold — used only for moments of insight and evidence. Researcher names in the KOL section. The "now I see it" revelatory moments in the result display. Archetype names on the result page. These are not decorative uses — they mark the moments the product delivers its core value.

**Depth accent:** Deep Dusk sections appear 1-2 times per page, for alternating rhythm and the most important content statements.

**Neutral fabric:** Stone and Soft Clay provide background texture for supporting UI elements, cards, and secondary content.

### What to Avoid

- **Purple in any form** — reads as clinical/therapeutic, immediately triggers "healing journey" associations. This is the single most important avoidance.
- **Bright amber or orange** — reads as Headspace derivative, energetic-wellness. The current `amber-600` should be retired completely.
- **Clinical white** — pure white (`#FFFFFF`) reads as clinical, tech, or institutional. Use bone and soft clay instead.
- **Pastel multicolor** — signals beginner-accessibility and entertainment (Wondermind register). One chromatic accent, not many.
- **Teal** — associated with the clinical-warmth register of therapy platforms (Attachment Project, BetterHelp). Even warm teal reads as "I should see a therapist."
- **Neon or saturated brights** — incompatible with depth register entirely.

## Typography Direction

### Current State Assessment

**Cormorant Garamond** (display) + **Geist Sans** (body). Per 06-RESEARCH.md: "directionally strong — editorial warmth + modern legibility." The `--font-display` CSS variable is established in the codebase. The pairing pattern aligns with all ten reference brands in the gallery — editorial serif display, clean humanist sans body.

### Display Font Evaluation: Cormorant Garamond

**Testing against the positioning territory:**

"Grounded self-discovery" requires a display font that communicates:
1. Depth without academic distance — the font should feel warm and present, not like a research paper
2. Editorial credibility without institutional coldness — bookish in the best sense, not governmental
3. Intimacy — a typeface that speaks to you as an adult, not at you as a patient or a student

**Cormorant Garamond evaluated:**

Cormorant has calligraphic roots, high stroke contrast, generous descenders, and an italic form of unusual elegance. It is specifically designed to bridge old-world editorial depth and contemporary typographic usage. In the reference brand pattern synthesis: "contemporary editorial serif — high stroke contrast, generous ascenders, elegant cap height" is the consistent description of what works for this register.

**The italic form of Cormorant specifically** is its most distinctive feature. The swash capitals, the deeply inclined text, and the delicate hairline strokes in italic create an emotional expressiveness that few display fonts achieve. This form is currently underused in the Raising Giants implementation.

**Alternatives considered:**
- **Canela** (type.weltanschauung.com): High stroke contrast, fashionable in premium branding. Strong candidate. More contemporary than Cormorant — reads as "editorial 2024" versus Cormorant's "editorial timeless." Canela might feel more immediately modern but has less emotional depth than Cormorant at close reading.
- **Freight Display** (GarageFonts): Warm, approachable serif. Excellent for intimate editorial use. Slightly more humanist than Cormorant — better for long-form reading; potentially less expressive for headlines.
- **Playfair Display** (Google Fonts): The most common editorial serif on the web. Because of its ubiquity, it has lost its distinctiveness — it reads as "I chose a fancy serif" rather than as a specific typographic voice.

**Recommendation: Keep Cormorant Garamond.**

The reasons against changing: (1) The 06-02-A decision confirms it as the correct font — "aligned with all high-depth reference brands." (2) Changing the display font would produce marginal differentiation while introducing implementation cost. (3) The italic form of Cormorant is underutilized — the brief should specify increased use of the italic for emotional emphasis, not a replacement of the font. The font's full potential has not been reached in the current implementation.

What should change is the *application* of Cormorant, not the font itself:
- The italic form should be used for pull quotes, archetype names in context, and emotional emphasis statements (not just for decoration)
- Headline sizing hierarchy should increase — larger display headlines (for the landing page hero, for section openers) and smaller body type, creating a more pronounced hierarchy that signals editorial confidence
- Uppercase tracking should be avoided — Cormorant's elegance comes from its lowercase proportions; all-caps tracking fights the font's natural qualities

### Body Font Evaluation: Geist Sans

**Testing against the positioning territory:**

Body text in this brand register must: (1) disappear into readability so the content carries the weight, (2) pair without competing with Cormorant's editorial warmth, (3) support long-form result content that users will read carefully.

**Geist Sans evaluated:**

Geist is Vercel's open-source font — a clean, geometric-inflected sans-serif with excellent rendering at small sizes, designed for developer interfaces. It is legible, neutral, and technically well-crafted. For reading product descriptions, quiz questions, and short labels, it works well.

For long-form result content — the archetype profile, the theme paragraphs, the citation-backed insights — Geist may produce slightly less comfortable reading than a humanist sans-serif (which has more organic proportions that reduce reading fatigue over extended reading sessions).

**The concern:** If the result page contains 800-1200 word archetype profiles, Geist's slight geometric rigidity may introduce reading friction that a humanist alternative (Inter, DM Sans, Atkinson Hyperlegible) would not.

**Recommendation: Keep Geist Sans with a specific carve-out.**

For marketing copy, quiz interface, and labels: Geist Sans performs well. Keep.

For long-form result page content (archetype profile body text, theme paragraphs, citation text): consider switching to **DM Sans** or **Inter** (both are Google Fonts, available via `next/font/google`) for the extended reading context only. This is a low-cost change (one font addition, different CSS variable assignment) that significantly improves the reading experience for the product's most important content delivery moment.

If this feels like unnecessary complexity: Geist can stay across the board. The important recommendation is that if user testing reveals fatigue with result page reading, body font humanization is the correct intervention.

### Type Scale and Treatment Notes

**Display hierarchy:**
- Hero headline: 72-96px Cormorant Garamond regular or italic. Not bold — Cormorant's weight is in its stroke contrast, not its weight class.
- Section openers: 48-64px Cormorant regular. First line of each major section.
- Subheadings: 28-36px Cormorant italic — the italic form is the brand's warmest typographic gesture; subheadings in italic signal "you are now entering a personal, direct conversation."
- Pull quotes / emotional emphasis: 24-32px Cormorant italic, centered, generous line height (1.8). This is the typography of "I always sensed this, now I see it" — slow, deliberate, present.

**Body hierarchy:**
- Primary body text: 17-18px Geist/DM Sans at 1.7-1.85 line height, measure 65-75 characters per line
- Supporting text: 15-16px at 1.6 line height
- Labels and metadata: 13-14px, tracked slightly, stone-500 color

**Spacing:** The pattern synthesis is clear — 1.6-1.85 line height for body, constrained measure (65-80 characters). The current implementation likely violates this in wide viewports — the execution plan should set max-width constraints that enforce this measure.

**What to avoid:**
- All-caps Cormorant — fights the font's lowercase proportions
- Bold Cormorant — the font is not designed for bold weight; it creates chunky, ungraceful forms
- Cormorant at small sizes (below 22px) — the hairline strokes disappear at small display sizes; below this threshold use Geist/DM Sans only
