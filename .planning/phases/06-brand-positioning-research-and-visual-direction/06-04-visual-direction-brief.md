# Visual Direction Brief
## Phase 06-04 — Translating Grounded Self-Discovery into Visual Language

**Date:** 2026-02-27
**Status:** Complete
**Positioning anchor:** The brand occupies the 'grounded self-discovery' territory — deep + warm, research-credible but emotionally intimate. The visual identity must make someone feel 'this understands me' before they read a word. Every visual choice must be testable against the emotional core: "I always sensed this. Now I see it."
**Feeds into:** 06-05-PLAN.md (rebrand execution plan)

---

## Positioning Anchor

The brand territory is grounded self-discovery — the intersection of clinical credibility and emotional intimacy. "Grounded" carries three simultaneous meanings: research-backed (the insight comes from clinical science, not astrology), the past as foundation (the product is about the ground on which you were built), and not abstract (specific, connected to reality). "Self-discovery" specifies the inward orientation — the adult reflecting on their own upbringing, not prescribing anything to others.

The emotional proposition is "I always sensed this. Now I see it." This is revelation, not validation or improvement. The product shows you something that was always true — it does not improve you, comfort you, or give you a type you can share. This creates a very specific visual mandate: the identity must communicate depth, intimacy, and a kind of earned intelligence — not wellness warmth, not clinical authority, not entertainment.

The brand is not: therapy (no healing journey language). Not wellness (no habits or practices). Not parenting advice (no prescriptive outward guidance). Not a personality quiz (not entertaining, not shareable-type-adjacent). Every visual choice that risks reading as any of these four categories must be reconsidered.

## Color Palette Direction

### Current State Assessment

The current palette uses:
- Primary backgrounds: `amber-50` (warm cream, ~`#FFFBEB`), `white`, `stone-900` (deep charcoal, ~`#1C1917`)
- Accent: `amber-600` (~`#D97706`) / `amber-700` (~`#B45309`) / `amber-400` (~`#FBBF24`)
- Text: `stone-900`, `stone-600`, `stone-500`

Per the 06-RESEARCH.md assessment: "reads 'startup warm neutral' rather than 'emotionally distinctive.'" The amber is functional but not specifically meaningful — it doesn't communicate anything unique about this product's territory. Warmth is there; specificity is not. The palette is directionally correct (non-clinical, non-purple, non-neon) but lacks a chromatic anchor that carries the emotional weight of "grounded self-discovery."

### Evaluating the Two Paths

**Path A: Deepen and differentiate the existing amber palette**

Strategy: Replace the generic amber with a more specific, emotionally charged variant — aged gold or deep amber — and introduce a secondary warm accent to differentiate from generic startup palettes. Preserves continuity with existing brand equity (however nascent), minimal disruption to codebase.

Evidence from reference brands: The School of Life uses deep mustard/golden yellow as a signifier of "considered time and intellectual depth." Day One uses warm gold as a "book spine" color. Neither is Tailwind's `amber` family — both are richer, more saturated, and carry more historical/emotional resonance.

Verdict: Path A can work, but only if the amber is *deepened* from the current `amber-600` (which reads startup) to a more specific aged-gold that carries genuine semantic weight. The distinction between Tailwind amber and aged-gold is not aesthetic — it is the difference between "warm" and "warm with a specific emotional claim."

**Path B: Shift to a more distinctive chromatic identity**

Strategy: Move the accent color entirely to a different hue — most likely terracotta/clay, which carries unique semantic content for this brand territory: earthy, past-oriented, enduring, specific.

Evidence from reference brands: The Gottman Institute uses warm terracotta as its human/earthy accent alongside a clinical-warmth neutral. In the color pattern synthesis across all 10 brands, "terracotta (the earthen, the past, the enduring)" is identified as one of the strongest candidates for a brand whose territory is "looking back at the ground on which you were built." Terracotta specifically encodes: soil, clay, something fired and permanent, the physical substance of the earth. For a product about understanding the formative conditions of your upbringing — the literal ground you were built on — this is not a decorative choice but a semantic one.

Verdict: Path B is the stronger recommendation based on what the positioning narrative establishes. The positioning is about the past as foundation, about the ground on which you stand. Terracotta is the most semantically direct chromatic encoding of this territory.

### Recommendation: Path B with Amber Background Continuity

Keep the warm cream/ivory backgrounds (which are working and are not generic) and the deep stone/charcoal text (which reads as intelligent depth, not clinical coldness). Replace amber accent with terracotta as the primary chromatic accent. This preserves what is working (the warm neutral base, the editorial depth of the near-black) while introducing a chromatic signal that is specific to the brand's positioning rather than generic to the "warm startup" category.

### Recommended Palette

**1. Bone (primary background)**
- Hex: `#F7F2EB`
- Role: Primary page background, card backgrounds
- Emotional rationale: Warmer and more aged than Tailwind amber-50. Reads as parchment, aged paper — the material substance of memory and record. "Bone" is neither clinical white nor ostentatiously warm. It has the texture of something that has been here a long time, which is what the product is about.

**2. Charcoal (primary text)**
- Hex: `#1C1512`
- Role: Primary text, headlines, high-contrast elements
- Emotional rationale: Slightly warmer than Tailwind stone-900 (which has a cool undertone). Near-black with warmth communicates: serious, grounded, present — not institutional, not clinical. The warmth prevents reading as tech product; the depth prevents reading as soft wellness.

**3. Terracotta (primary accent)**
- Hex: `#B05A3A`
- Role: Primary CTAs, active states, accent marks, key typographic emphases
- Emotional rationale: This is the semantic anchor of the palette. Terracotta is earth that has been fired — permanent, specific, past. For a brand about understanding the conditions that formed you, terracotta is the most direct chromatic claim: this product is about the ground. It is not orange (energetic, startup), not red (urgent, emotional), not amber (generic warm). Terracotta is grounded.

**4. Aged Gold (secondary accent)**
- Hex: `#C4892A`
- Role: Highlight states, supporting decorative elements, researcher/evidence emphasis
- Emotional rationale: Where terracotta is the past (the earth), aged gold is the accumulated understanding (the insight). Gold implies something that has been earned over time — wisdom, not luck. Used for secondary emphasis: researcher names, archetype labels, "finally seeing" moments in the product. Distinct from Tailwind amber because it reads as earned, not applied.

**5. Stone (supporting neutrals)**
- Hex: `#7A6E65` (mid), `#B8AFA6` (light)
- Role: Body text on light backgrounds, muted labels, supporting hierarchy
- Emotional rationale: Stone is the tonal background of the palette — not warm enough to be earthy, not cool enough to be clinical. The mid and light stone tones provide hierarchy without competing with terracotta and gold for chromatic attention.

**6. Deep Dusk (section alternate background)**
- Hex: `#1E1814`
- Role: Dark sections for alternating layout rhythm, high-contrast hero moments
- Emotional rationale: A deeper, warmer version of the current stone-900. Used for sections where the product speaks with its most authoritative, intimate voice — "here is what we know about your upbringing." The warmth prevents the dark from reading as tech-dark or premium-cold. On deep dusk backgrounds: text in bone, accent in aged gold.

**7. Soft Clay (secondary surface)**
- Hex: `#EBE0D5`
- Role: Card surfaces, secondary section backgrounds, subtle differentiation from primary bone
- Emotional rationale: A slightly darker variant of bone — clay, not parchment. Creates depth in the layout without introducing new chromatic hues. Communicates the same earthen, analog quality as bone but with more presence.

### Color Relationships and Rhythm

**Dominant:** Bone (background), Charcoal (text) — the reading surface. These appear on every page and should feel like the natural visual air of the brand.

**Structural accent:** Terracotta — used sparingly but consistently as the single chromatic through-line. One element per section maximum. Never background; always foreground element (button, underline, archetype label, icon).

**Earned accent:** Aged Gold — used only for moments of insight and evidence. Researcher names in the KOL section. The "now I see it" revelatory moments in the result display. Archetype names on the result page. These are not decorative uses — they mark the moments the product delivers its core value.

**Depth accent:** Deep Dusk sections appear 1-2 times per page, for alternating rhythm and the most important content statements.

**Neutral fabric:** Stone and Soft Clay provide background texture for supporting UI elements, cards, and secondary content.

### What to Avoid

- **Purple in any form** — reads as clinical/therapeutic, immediately triggers "healing journey" associations. This is the single most important avoidance.
- **Bright amber or orange** — reads as Headspace derivative, energetic-wellness. The current `amber-600` should be retired completely.
- **Clinical white** — pure white (`#FFFFFF`) reads as clinical, tech, or institutional. Use bone and soft clay instead.
- **Pastel multicolor** — signals beginner-accessibility and entertainment (Wondermind register). One chromatic accent, not many.
- **Teal** — associated with the clinical-warmth register of therapy platforms (Attachment Project, BetterHelp). Even warm teal reads as "I should see a therapist."
- **Neon or saturated brights** — incompatible with depth register entirely.

## Typography Direction

### Current State Assessment

**Cormorant Garamond** (display) + **Geist Sans** (body). Per 06-RESEARCH.md: "directionally strong — editorial warmth + modern legibility." The `--font-display` CSS variable is established in the codebase. The pairing pattern aligns with all ten reference brands in the gallery — editorial serif display, clean humanist sans body.

### Display Font Evaluation: Cormorant Garamond

**Testing against the positioning territory:**

"Grounded self-discovery" requires a display font that communicates:
1. Depth without academic distance — the font should feel warm and present, not like a research paper
2. Editorial credibility without institutional coldness — bookish in the best sense, not governmental
3. Intimacy — a typeface that speaks to you as an adult, not at you as a patient or a student

**Cormorant Garamond evaluated:**

Cormorant has calligraphic roots, high stroke contrast, generous descenders, and an italic form of unusual elegance. It is specifically designed to bridge old-world editorial depth and contemporary typographic usage. In the reference brand pattern synthesis: "contemporary editorial serif — high stroke contrast, generous ascenders, elegant cap height" is the consistent description of what works for this register.

**The italic form of Cormorant specifically** is its most distinctive feature. The swash capitals, the deeply inclined text, and the delicate hairline strokes in italic create an emotional expressiveness that few display fonts achieve. This form is currently underused in the Raising Giants implementation.

**Alternatives considered:**
- **Canela** (type.weltanschauung.com): High stroke contrast, fashionable in premium branding. Strong candidate. More contemporary than Cormorant — reads as "editorial 2024" versus Cormorant's "editorial timeless." Canela might feel more immediately modern but has less emotional depth than Cormorant at close reading.
- **Freight Display** (GarageFonts): Warm, approachable serif. Excellent for intimate editorial use. Slightly more humanist than Cormorant — better for long-form reading; potentially less expressive for headlines.
- **Playfair Display** (Google Fonts): The most common editorial serif on the web. Because of its ubiquity, it has lost its distinctiveness — it reads as "I chose a fancy serif" rather than as a specific typographic voice.

**Recommendation: Keep Cormorant Garamond.**

The reasons against changing: (1) The 06-02-A decision confirms it as the correct font — "aligned with all high-depth reference brands." (2) Changing the display font would produce marginal differentiation while introducing implementation cost. (3) The italic form of Cormorant is underutilized — the brief should specify increased use of the italic for emotional emphasis, not a replacement of the font. The font's full potential has not been reached in the current implementation.

What should change is the *application* of Cormorant, not the font itself:
- The italic form should be used for pull quotes, archetype names in context, and emotional emphasis statements (not just for decoration)
- Headline sizing hierarchy should increase — larger display headlines (for the landing page hero, for section openers) and smaller body type, creating a more pronounced hierarchy that signals editorial confidence
- Uppercase tracking should be avoided — Cormorant's elegance comes from its lowercase proportions; all-caps tracking fights the font's natural qualities

### Body Font Evaluation: Geist Sans

**Testing against the positioning territory:**

Body text in this brand register must: (1) disappear into readability so the content carries the weight, (2) pair without competing with Cormorant's editorial warmth, (3) support long-form result content that users will read carefully.

**Geist Sans evaluated:**

Geist is Vercel's open-source font — a clean, geometric-inflected sans-serif with excellent rendering at small sizes, designed for developer interfaces. It is legible, neutral, and technically well-crafted. For reading product descriptions, quiz questions, and short labels, it works well.

For long-form result content — the archetype profile, the theme paragraphs, the citation-backed insights — Geist may produce slightly less comfortable reading than a humanist sans-serif (which has more organic proportions that reduce reading fatigue over extended reading sessions).

**The concern:** If the result page contains 800-1200 word archetype profiles, Geist's slight geometric rigidity may introduce reading friction that a humanist alternative (Inter, DM Sans, Atkinson Hyperlegible) would not.

**Recommendation: Keep Geist Sans with a specific carve-out.**

For marketing copy, quiz interface, and labels: Geist Sans performs well. Keep.

For long-form result page content (archetype profile body text, theme paragraphs, citation text): consider switching to **DM Sans** or **Inter** (both are Google Fonts, available via `next/font/google`) for the extended reading context only. This is a low-cost change (one font addition, different CSS variable assignment) that significantly improves the reading experience for the product's most important content delivery moment.

If this feels like unnecessary complexity: Geist can stay across the board. The important recommendation is that if user testing reveals fatigue with result page reading, body font humanization is the correct intervention.

### Type Scale and Treatment Notes

**Display hierarchy:**
- Hero headline: 72-96px Cormorant Garamond regular or italic. Not bold — Cormorant's weight is in its stroke contrast, not its weight class.
- Section openers: 48-64px Cormorant regular. First line of each major section.
- Subheadings: 28-36px Cormorant italic — the italic form is the brand's warmest typographic gesture; subheadings in italic signal "you are now entering a personal, direct conversation."
- Pull quotes / emotional emphasis: 24-32px Cormorant italic, centered, generous line height (1.8). This is the typography of "I always sensed this, now I see it" — slow, deliberate, present.

**Body hierarchy:**
- Primary body text: 17-18px Geist/DM Sans at 1.7-1.85 line height, measure 65-75 characters per line
- Supporting text: 15-16px at 1.6 line height
- Labels and metadata: 13-14px, tracked slightly, stone-500 color

**Spacing:** The pattern synthesis is clear — 1.6-1.85 line height for body, constrained measure (65-80 characters). The current implementation likely violates this in wide viewports — the execution plan should set max-width constraints that enforce this measure.

**What to avoid:**
- All-caps Cormorant — fights the font's lowercase proportions
- Bold Cormorant — the font is not designed for bold weight; it creates chunky, ungraceful forms
- Cormorant at small sizes (below 22px) — the hairline strokes disappear at small display sizes; below this threshold use Geist/DM Sans only

## Imagery / Illustration System

### Current State

The brand currently uses three custom SVG illustration categories:
- **MirrorIllustration** — abstract/geometric hero image for the landing page
- **ArchetypeIcons** — simplified geometric icons for each of the 9 archetypes
- **KOL portrait sketches** — hand-drawn line portraits of the 8 featured researchers

The style across all three is abstract-geometric: clean lines, reduced detail, no characters or scenes. This approach is strategically correct (illustration over stock photography, for a product about interior psychological states) but style-incorrect for the target emotional register.

### Direction Recommendation: Illustration-Based with Emotional Metaphor Style

**The choice:** Character/illustration-based, not documentary photography, not pure abstraction.

**Rationale from positioning:** The product is about interior psychological states — the experience of recognizing patterns from your upbringing, the feeling of finally naming something you've always sensed. Photography captures exterior reality; pure geometric abstraction decorates without communicating. What is needed is illustration that can depict *interior states and psychological relationships* in a way that is specific enough to feel like it was made for this person, not generic enough to be a stock image.

**Evidence from reference brands:**

The School of Life's line-art illustration system is the strongest template. It depicts everyday human situations from the inside — a person alone in a room, two people in a complex moment, a figure looking at something only they can see. The drawings show interior states, not exterior moments. The style is: black or sepia line on ivory ground, minimal detail, emotionally specific posture and gesture, no faces needed (the absence of faces allows the viewer to project themselves into the scene).

Getrelatio.com uses illustration specifically for abstract concepts (connection, understanding, compatibility) — not for depicting real people. The illustrations signal "conceptual, thoughtful" rather than "stock-like."

**Recommended style:** Editorial line illustration in the tradition of The School of Life — ink line on bone ground, moderate line weight (not thin and spidery, not thick and graphic), emotional specificity in gesture and posture, metaphorical rather than literal subject matter. Characters should feel like sketches from a trusted friend's notebook, not brand mascots or clip-art.

### Illustration Style Notes

**Line weight:** Moderate — confident without being heavy. The line should feel like it was drawn by hand with intention, not generated by a machine or traced from a vector template. Reference: the visual quality of an editorial illustration in a literary magazine.

**Color usage within illustrations:** Maximum two colors — charcoal line on bone ground, with occasional terracotta accent (a single element: a doorway, a mirror frame, a highlighted gesture). Colored fill should be rare and meaningful. The restraint of the color usage is itself a brand signal: this is not playful, not colorful, not entertainment.

**Level of detail:** Low to moderate. The illustration should communicate its metaphor clearly without explaining it. A figure reflected in water needs to convey "reflection" — not a photorealist rendering of ripples. The School of Life's figures can be recognized as emotional states in two or three lines.

**Emotional register:** Intimate, reflective, quiet. The illustrations should feel like they depict a private moment — something that would feel uncomfortable to see in a busy social context, natural in a moment of solitude. This registers exactly as "grounded self-discovery."

**What to avoid:** Abstract geometric shapes (current style — reads as decorative), illustration characters with visible personality quirks (reads as mascot/brand character, like Headspace), photorealism, stock illustration style, and 3D rendering of any kind.

### Hero / Key Visual Concept for the Landing Page

**The brief:** The hero illustration (replacing or evolving MirrorIllustration) should communicate the product's core emotional proposition without text support. A person looking at the image should sense "recognition," "looking at something about oneself," and "the past being made visible" — before reading any copy.

**Recommended visual metaphor:** A figure looking into a reflecting surface (water, a window, an actual mirror) that shows not a face but a scene — specifically, a scene from childhood or from the past. The figure in the present is looking at a reflection that is also looking back. This captures: self-understanding, the past made visible, the revelation of something that was always there.

Alternative concepts that serve the metaphor:
- A figure reading a document, and the document's shadow behind them reveals the scene of a childhood home
- Two overlapping circles of light — one from above (the present), one from behind (the past) — meeting at the point where the figure stands
- A hand reaching backward to touch a mark left on a wall — the mark remains, the gesture is acknowledgment

**Implementation note for execution plan:** The current MirrorIllustration should be redesigned with this conceptual brief. The SVG can remain (low implementation cost); the visual concept should change from abstract geometric to emotionally metaphorical narrative.

### Photography Guidelines

The visual direction is primarily illustration-based. However, if photography is used (for social media, email, or future content), the guidelines are:

**Subject matter:** Hands (writing, holding objects, reaching). Unguarded faces in private moments (reading, thinking, alone with a feeling). Objects with history (worn notebooks, aged objects, photographs being held). Not: lifestyle photography, nature-as-metaphor (sunrises, mountains), couples or group shots, posed smiling.

**Lighting:** Natural, interior, directed. The quality of afternoon light through a window — warm, specific, not dramatic. Avoid: studio lighting, golden-hour saturation, moody high-contrast, ring light.

**Color grading:** Warm, slightly desaturated, slightly aged. Not vintage-filter obvious, but not contemporary crisp. The photographs should feel like they could be from any decade — that timeless quality is its own credibility signal.

**Composition:** Intimate scale. Close framing. White space around the subject. Not: environmental portraits with the subject small in a big scene, dynamic composition, burst-energy angles.

## Layout Approach

### Spaciousness vs. Density

This brand needs generous whitespace as a trust signal. The pattern synthesis from all 10 reference brands is unambiguous: "whitespace is the most reliable signal of depth in the target audience's experience." Brands that created the "deep" feeling used significantly more whitespace than brands that felt shallow.

The target for Raising Giants: closer to Kinfolk than to Headspace. Not the extreme silence of Kinfolk, which would lose warmth — but 30-50% more whitespace per section than the current implementation. Whitespace in this context is not empty space: it is the typographic equivalent of the space between questions in a slow, meaningful conversation. It communicates: "we are not rushing you. This is worth your time."

**Practical specification:** Section vertical padding should be a minimum of 80px on mobile, 120px on desktop. Above-the-fold content should show no more than 60% of the viewport — enough to establish the hero, invite curiosity, and leave room for the page to breathe.

### Editorial vs. App-Like

The brand must sit at the editorial end of the spectrum. "App-like" layout — grid-based, card-heavy, feature-list-organized — is consistently associated with the "warm but shallow" competitors in the reference research. The correct register is the layout of a beautifully designed book that happens to exist on screen.

**Specific prohibitions:**
- No feature-list cards (grid of 3 or 6 benefit cards) — this is the startup quiz template pattern
- No progress indicators styled as games (badge counts, stars, percentages as accomplishments)
- No persistent sticky CTAs that appear at scroll-start — these signal "we want your conversion more than your experience"
- No section-level dividers that look like tabs or menus

**Specific recommendations:**
- Text sections should run to their natural length — not cut off by "read more" links that prevent reading
- CTA placement should feel natural, not aggressive — it should appear when the user has received enough to know they want to act
- The quiz interface should feel like a private questionnaire, not a game or a survey — no animation between questions, no gamification signals, no progress bar that celebrates completion

### Section Rhythm

Each section should have a defined role in an editorial sequence. The rhythm pattern:

1. **Opening narrative section** — text-heavy, generous whitespace, Cormorant display headline. Establishes the emotional territory. No images competing.
2. **Visual breathing section** — image-dominant or illustration-anchor, minimal text, primarily white/bone surface. Allows the reader to rest and let the previous section settle.
3. **Evidence section** — structured (researcher names, research citations, specific claims) with aged gold typography treatment. Provides the research grounding without institutional coldness.
4. **Intimate testimony section** — pull quotes in Cormorant italic, personal framing. This is the vocabulary-gift moment — the section where users recognize themselves.
5. **Transition + CTA section** — returns to text, introduces the quiz or next product, lands the decision in the emotional context established by the preceding sequence.

This sequence should repeat once per scroll-length of the landing page — approximately every 3-4 viewport heights.

### Key Layout Principle

**"Generous negative space that creates room for reflection."**

This is the overarching layout principle derived from the positioning. The product asks users to reflect on difficult, intimate, and formative experiences. The layout must create the visual environment in which that reflection feels possible. A crowded, rushing layout is antithetical to the emotional state the product requires. The negative space is not nothing — it is the visual permission to think slowly, to stay with something, to let recognition settle.

## Brand Tone / Voice Direction

### Voice Register

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Warm ←→ Clinical | 8/10 toward Warm | Never cold, never prescriptive. Warm in the specific sense of "speaking directly to you" — not "warm and uplifting." |
| Personal (you) ←→ Observational | 9/10 toward Personal (you) | "You" consistently. Not "many adults," not "some people," not "research suggests that individuals." Second-person direct address is non-negotiable. |
| Poetic ←→ Plain | 4/10 toward Poetic | More poetic than clinical, less poetic than purple-prose. The language should feel like a thoughtful adult speaking to another thoughtful adult. Not flowery. Not clinical. Considered. |
| Confident ←→ Tentative | 8/10 toward Confident | Confident that the framework is grounded and the insight is real. Tentative only in the sense of "this may have looked like X" — the product respects that it is making a claim about someone's experience, not dictating it. |

### Permission-First Voice Pattern

Before any revelation, give permission. This pattern appears across Brené Brown, On Being, and getrelatio.com — it creates psychological safety for depth.

**Pattern:**
1. Name what the user might be sensing before they have language for it
2. Normalize: confirm that this sensing is common, real, and worth attending to
3. Deliver the named concept as vocabulary gift: "There's a name for this: ___"
4. Invite into the product from a position of trust already established

**Example of pattern in practice:**

"You've probably spent years aware that something about how you grew up shaped who you are. The way you react under pressure. What makes you feel safe — or suddenly, inexplicably, not. You've felt it without quite having words for it. The archetype framework gives you those words."

This passage: names the sensed experience (years of awareness), normalizes it (no explanation needed — the user already knows this), delivers the vocabulary-gift promise, and invites action without pressure.

### Example Copy Transformations

**Current copy (approximate):** "Take a 5-minute quiz to discover your parenting archetype"

**Problem:** "Discover" implies novelty not recognition. "Parenting archetype" implies the product is about parenting. "5 minutes" prioritizes efficiency over depth. Reads as: quiz app, lead magnet.

**Rewritten in recommended voice:** "In 21 questions, the Mirror shows you the archetype that shaped the way you were raised — and the patterns you've been carrying since."

What changed: "Mirror" as verb (active, product-as-action, not quiz-as-quiz). "Shaped the way you were raised" (inward, past-lens, specific). "Patterns you've been carrying" (permission-giving — this is about you, and you've been living with these patterns already).

---

**Current copy (approximate):** "Understand your parenting style and break generational patterns"

**Problem:** "Your parenting style" — outward-facing, assumes the user is a parent, advisory. "Break generational patterns" — therapeutic/healing journey framing ("break" implies damage). Reads as: parenting advice, therapy-adjacent.

**Rewritten in recommended voice:** "See clearly what shaped you. Not to change yourself — to finally understand why you are the way you are."

What changed: "See clearly" (revelation framing, not advice). "Not to change yourself" (explicitly addresses the therapy-avoidance positioning — this is not about fixing you). "Finally understand why you are the way you are" (the core emotional proposition — recognition of what was always true).

---

**Current copy (approximate):** "Get your free result"

**Problem:** "Get" is transactional. "Free result" sounds like a lead-magnet deliverable. Reads as: generic quiz funnel.

**Rewritten in recommended voice:** "Take the Mirror"

What changed: "Take" is active — you are doing something intentional, not receiving a product. "The Mirror" as the product name — not "quiz," not "test," not "assessment." The product has a name; use it. Shorter. More confident. Sounds like an invitation, not an offer.

### Forbidden Patterns

These copy patterns must be banned from all primary brand communication:

**1. Parenting advice framing:**
- "How to raise ___"
- "Better parent" / "improve your parenting"
- "Your child needs ___"
- "What your kids are trying to tell you"

**2. Therapeutic / healing journey framing:**
- "Break the cycle"
- "Heal your relationship with ___"
- "Trauma" as a primary positioning word (can appear in context within the product but not in primary brand messaging)
- "Work on yourself" / "do the work"
- "Processing" / "working through"

**3. Quiz / entertainment framing:**
- "Find out which ___ you are"
- "Share your result"
- "Only X% of people get ___"
- Percentage scores, compatibility ratings, type comparisons

**4. Wellness / practice framing:**
- "Daily practice" / "ritual" / "habit"
- "Journey" (except in very specific contexts where it is clearly a personal insight journey, not a wellness journey)
- "Transform" / "transformation"
- "Empower" / "empowerment"

**5. Aspiration / urgency framing:**
- "Don't miss out"
- "Finally be the parent / partner / person you want to be"
- "Unlock your potential"
- "The quiz that changed everything" (testimonial language that sounds hyperbolic)

## Mood Reference

A user arrives on the landing page. The background is a warm cream-bone — not white, not beige, but parchment — and the first thing they see is a large Cormorant Garamond headline in deep warm charcoal, set loosely with generous line height: *"You've always sensed something about how you were raised."* The type is large enough that it asks to be read slowly. There is space around it — real space, not filler — so it sits on the page the way a first sentence sits at the beginning of a book.

Below the headline, a single illustration: a figure in line-art on the bone ground, drawn in warm charcoal ink with a single terracotta accent — a doorframe, or a reflected surface — that draws the eye without demanding it. The figure is in a position of recognition, not action: looking at something, registering something. The illustration is not trying to be beautiful. It is trying to be specific.

As the user scrolls, the first section gives them language they recognize: named experiences, vocabulary for things they've carried without names. The terracotta accent appears on archetype labels — "The Intentional Guide," "The Resilient Striver" — punctuating the text the way a teacher underlines a key term. This is not decoration. This is the vocabulary gift arriving.

A darker section — deep dusk — interrupts the warmth, as the product transitions to its research grounding. On the dark background, the researcher names appear in aged gold: Bowlby, Ainsworth, Gottman, Siegel. The gold reads as earned authority, not institutional credential. Below each name, a brief description in bone-colored type that treats the researcher as a human being doing important work, not a citation in a bibliography.

The result of this sequence — reading unhurried, moving through sections that alternate between text and breathing space, encountering an illustration that shows you something interior — is a feeling that precedes action: *this was made for me. This knows something about me that I haven't told it.*

The overall emotional outcome: the feeling a thoughtful adult gets when they encounter something that meets them at the level of their actual sophistication. Not surprised that psychology products can be this nuanced. Not relieved that it's not intimidating. Not entertained. *Recognized.*
