## Decisions Made

**[06-04-A] Palette chromatic shift: terracotta replaces amber**
The competitive analysis and reference brand pattern synthesis both identify the current amber as "reads startup warm neutral rather than emotionally distinctive." Terracotta was selected over amber deepening because it carries specific semantic content for the positioning territory: earthen, past-oriented, the material substance of foundation. The three other palette elements (bone background, charcoal text, aged gold secondary accent) were specified to reinforce this semantic anchor rather than compete with it.

**[06-04-B] Cormorant Garamond: keep with application upgrades**
The font is correctly chosen — confirmed by alignment with 8 of 10 reference brands using editorial serif display. The issue is underutilization: the italic form, which is unusually elegant and emotionally expressive in Cormorant, is not currently used for emotional emphasis, pull quotes, or subheadings. The brief specifies increased italic use as the primary typography upgrade rather than a font change.

**[06-04-C] Illustration system: editorial line over abstract geometric**
The reference brand synthesis consistently shows that illustration depicting interior psychological states (The School of Life's line-art, getrelatio's conceptual illustration) creates the "seen" feeling more effectively than abstract geometric decoration. The current SVG illustrations are abstract — they decorate rather than communicate. The brief specifies the shift to emotionally metaphorical, figure-based editorial illustration with the artistic reference being The School of Life's approach: specific posture/gesture, minimal detail, interior-state legible.

**[06-04-D] Layout: whitespace as trust signal, editorial rhythm**
The pattern synthesis is unambiguous: whitespace is the most consistent depth signal across all ten reference brands. The brief mandates a 30-50% increase in section padding and specifies a four-type section rhythm (narrative → visual rest → evidence → testimony → CTA) that mirrors the alternating text/breath pattern of the highest-trust brands in the research.

**[06-04-E] Voice: permission-first pattern as required convention**
The permission-first tonal pattern appears in Brené Brown, On Being, and getrelatio — the three brands most successful at creating the "seen" feeling in self-reflective adults. The brief specifies this as a required convention with three worked examples: naming what the user has sensed, normalizing it, then delivering the vocabulary gift. The forbidden patterns section bans all five categories of copy that undermine the positioning (parenting advice, therapeutic journey, quiz entertainment, wellness practice, aspiration/urgency).
