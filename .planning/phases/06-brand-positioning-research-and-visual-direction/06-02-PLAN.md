**Required benchmark:**
- **getrelatio.com** — Analyze in depth as the primary benchmark per CONTEXT.md. Focus on: how they frame an assessment product as a meaningful personal experience rather than a throwaway lead magnet; the "modern and new" feel; consent-first language; community-driven social proof.

**Discovery approach — look across these categories:**
- Personal growth / self-help books or platforms (e.g., The School of Life, Brene Brown's brand)
- Therapy-adjacent content brands (e.g., therapy apps, mental health newsletters)
- Editorial / long-form storytelling brands (e.g., literary magazines, memoir publishers)
- Journaling / reflection tools (e.g., journaling apps, gratitude platforms)
- Wellness brands with depth (not surface-level wellness marketing)
- Podcast brands with intimate tone (e.g., psychology or relationship podcasts)

Do NOT limit to digital products. Include publishing brands, editorial brands, or content brands if their visual identity communicates the "grounded self-discovery" register.

For EACH reference brand, use WebFetch and WebSearch to document:

**Visual Attributes:**
1. Color palette — primary, secondary, accent colors (hex codes where possible)
2. Typography — display font, body font, style register (serif/sans/slab)
3. Imagery style — photography/illustration/abstract, mood, subject matter
4. Layout approach — spacious/dense, grid-based/organic, editorial/app-like
5. Distinctive elements — any unique visual signature (iconography, texture, pattern)

**Emotional Response:**
1. Primary emotion evoked — what do you feel first? (trust, curiosity, warmth, exclusivity, depth, calm, intimacy, intelligence)
2. Brand register — where on the spectrum: playful ←→ serious, accessible ←→ exclusive, clinical ←→ intimate
3. "Feels like" statement — complete the sentence "This brand feels like ___" (e.g., "a conversation with a wise friend," "a beautiful therapy waiting room," "a journal you write in every night")
4. What works — the 1-2 specific visual or tonal choices that produce the emotional response
5. What doesn't transfer — what about this brand would NOT work for Raising Giants and why

Format as a structured markdown document with a summary table at top and detailed profiles below.

<task type="auto">
  <name>Task 2: Synthesize visual and tonal patterns across reference brands</name>
  <files>.planning/phases/06-brand-positioning-research-and-visual-direction/06-02-reference-brand-gallery.md</files>
  <action>
Add a "Pattern Synthesis" section to the reference brand gallery document. This section synthesizes the recurring patterns across all profiled reference brands into actionable direction for the visual brief.

**Color Patterns:**
- What color families appear most in brands that communicate depth + warmth?
- Are there colors that consistently signal "trust" vs. "superficiality"?
- What palette strategies differentiate "grounded self-discovery" from generic wellness?

**Typography Patterns:**
- Do high-depth brands prefer serif or sans-serif for display?
- What body font styles accompany editorial/intimate display fonts?
- Is there a consistent relationship between font weight and emotional register?

**Imagery Patterns:**
- What imagery style appears in brands that create the "seen" feeling?
- Is illustration or photography more common in the "deep + warm" brands?
- What subject matter (people, nature, abstract, objects) dominates?

**Layout Patterns:**
- How much whitespace do "depth" brands use compared to "shallow" brands?
- What structural choices create intimacy vs. distance?
- Editorial vs. app-like — which is more common in the target register?

**Tone Patterns:**
- What copy style creates the "someone who understands me" feeling?
- First person, second person, or third person voice?
- Sentence length and complexity patterns?

Write the synthesis as a narrative (not just bullet points) with specific brand examples for each pattern. End with a "Direction Signals" section that distills the patterns into 5-7 actionable signals for the visual direction brief. Example signal format:

"**Signal: Serif display fonts communicate depth.** Brands that feel deep and intimate (e.g., [Brand A], [Brand B]) consistently use high-contrast serif display fonts. Sans-serif display fonts were associated with brands that felt more corporate or superficial."

This synthesis is the bridge between research and visual direction — it must be specific enough to guide concrete visual choices in Plan 04.
